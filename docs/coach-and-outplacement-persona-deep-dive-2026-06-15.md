## 1) Objective

Build a deep operating model for every major coach and outplacement sub-persona involved in executive job search journeys.

This document answers:
- The struggles, fears, behaviors, and decision points before, during, and after an executive job search.
- When coaches and outplacement leaders decide to start a search with clients.
- Who they contact, how they maintain relationships, and how they manage trust.
- How they and their clients build personal/professional brand and search narratives.
- How sessions are structured by persona: before, during, after, and between sessions.
- What each persona records and measures.
- Estimated market size: how many coaches/outplacement firms likely have clients searching now and this year.
- Information consumption behavior, outreach channels, and advertising opportunities.

## 2) Method and Sources

### Primary anchors used

- Internal strategy, persona, route, and runbook docs in this repository.
- BLS JOLTS (Apr 2026): total separations, quits, layoffs/discharges.
- BLS CPS occupation tables (management occupation levels).
- ICF 2025 Global Coaching Study summaries (coach count and growth direction).

### Modeling principles

- Prefer transparent scenario ranges over a single false-precision estimate.
- Separate:
  1. executive demand (people in transition),
  2. advisor supply (coaches/outplacement firms),
  3. channel reach (how many can be reached and activated).
- Explicitly call assumptions where direct census data does not exist.

## 3) System View: Who Exists in This Ecosystem

There are two major advisor channels:

1. Executive coaching channel
- Independent executive coach (solo)
- Boutique firm coach partner
- Enterprise-sponsored cohort coach lead
- Search-affiliate transition coach
- Board-positioning and governance coach

2. Outplacement channel
- Practice leader (P&L owner)
- Program operations director
- Counselor lead
- Counselor / transition advisor
- Procurement and legal reviewer
- Client HR/CHRO buyer counterpart

Across both channels, the executive client typically appears in one of three states:

1. Pre-search optionality mode (quiet monitoring)
2. Active campaign mode (high urgency)
3. Post-landing compounding mode (onboarding + reputation + next optionality)

## 4) Shared Behavioral Truths Across Channels

### 4.1 Core struggles

- Context rebuild drag: too much paid time spent reconstructing what happened since last touch.
- Inconsistent between-session execution: activity spikes around meetings, then drops.
- Narrative drift: story changes by audience and week.
- Signal blindness: executives react late because they only see public/late-stage demand.
- Accountability ambiguity: no shared definition of progress quality.

### 4.2 Core fears

Advisor fears:
- "I look replaceable if sessions become status recap."
- "My client is paying for strategy but getting admin."
- "I cannot prove impact with confidence."
- "A bad placement outcome harms referral reputation."

Executive fears (as seen by advisors):
- "My market value is falling while I wait."
- "I cannot explain my transition story cleanly."
- "I will accept the wrong role under pressure."
- "I cannot tell if a mandate is real or performative."

### 4.3 Shared goals

- Increase session strategic yield.
- Reduce time-to-first-quality-conversation.
- Improve offer quality, not only speed.
- Build repeatable, measured cadence.
- Preserve confidence and identity during uncertainty.

## 5) Deep Personas: Executive Coaches

## 5.1 Independent Executive Coach (Solo Operator)

### Profile

- 5-25 active clients/year.
- Revenue tied directly to utilization and referrals.
- High reliance on trust, intimacy, and bespoke guidance.
- Lowest operational support; highest personal admin burden.

### Before search (client pre-search optionality)

What they think:
- "This client is not ready to declare a search, but market optionality is collapsing if we wait too long."

What they do:
- Assess readiness signal: role fatigue, mandate mismatch, sponsor changes, stalled compensation trajectory.
- Push quiet preparation: narrative draft, target archetypes, relationship map.
- Encourage low-visibility signal monitoring to avoid reactive moves.

Who they reach out to:
- Select trusted recruiters (informal checks, no broad outreach).
- Past clients and peer coaches for market temperature.
- Sometimes one industry insider to test client story resonance.

Branding techniques:
- "Steady operator" framing for risk-sensitive clients.
- LinkedIn tune-up with subtle positioning shifts rather than overt search signals.

Narrative work:
- Build a three-layer narrative:
  1. Legacy (what was built/fixed),
  2. Inflection (why now),
  3. Next mandate (specific value in next seat).

### During active search

What they think:
- "If I let this become random activity, confidence and outcomes will both degrade."

What they do:
- Run weekly priority stack: top 5 target companies, top 10 relationships, top 2 narrative risks.
- Rehearse interview arcs by stakeholder (CEO, board, CHRO, peer).
- Tighten decision filters to avoid low-quality mandate traps.

Session structure:
- Before session:
  - Review what changed since last touch.
  - Flag stalled threads and missed follow-ups.
  - Pull upcoming conversation stakes.
- During session:
  - 10% state check (confidence, energy, fear).
  - 50% strategic decisions (targeting, messaging, sequencing).
  - 30% narrative rehearsal and objection handling.
  - 10% explicit commitments for next window.
- After session:
  - Send concise decision memo.
  - Confirm non-negotiable actions and deadlines.
- Between sessions:
  - Lightweight async nudges.
  - Risk intervention if no meaningful action for 5-7 days.

What they record:
- Opportunity stage changes.
- High-stakes conversation count.
- Narrative objection themes.
- Client confidence score trend.

What they measure:
- Session strategy-time ratio.
- Signal-to-action latency.
- First-interview velocity.
- Stalled pipeline ratio.

### After landing

What they do:
- 30/60/90 day onboarding narrative.
- Stakeholder trust map for first quarter.
- Post-search retrospective: what to keep for long-term optionality.

What they care about:
- Referral testimonial quality.
- Repeat engagement (board prep, next-level readiness).

## 5.2 Boutique Firm Coach Partner

### Profile

- Multi-coach practice, shared delivery standards.
- Needs consistency across coaches and cohorts.
- Balances craft quality with utilization economics.

### Before search

What they think:
- "Can we standardize readiness without flattening coach quality?"

What they do:
- Use common intake and readiness rubric.
- Segment clients into optionality/active/urgent tracks.
- Assign coach based on transition type and industry depth.

Who they reach out to:
- Alumni clients for ecosystem introductions.
- Selected retained recruiters.
- HR and outplacement partners for inbound flow.

Branding techniques:
- Practice-level thought leadership (framework-driven).
- Case-structured outcomes with conservative claims.

Narrative design:
- Standardized narrative template with persona-specific variants.
- Quality review checkpoints before market-facing use.

### During search

Session operations:
- Before:
  - Shared dashboard scan across active clients.
  - Identify high-risk clients for intervention.
- During:
  - Coach executes strategy + narrative + confidence management.
  - Firm standards enforced for action clarity and follow-through.
- After:
  - Update shared scorecard for partner visibility.
- Between:
  - Ops team monitors adoption and cadence adherence.

Recorded artifacts:
- Weekly client movement summary.
- Counselor notes on blockers.
- Narrative revision log.

Measured KPIs:
- Client activation within first 7 days.
- Week-2 signal-driven action rate.
- Prep-readiness before critical conversations.
- Placement quality indicators at 30/60/90 days.

### After search

- Roll client into alumni/referral engine.
- Capture named or anonymized proof assets.
- Feed lessons into playbook updates.

## 5.3 Enterprise-Sponsored Coach Cohort Lead

### Profile

- Coaching delivered in sponsored programs (HR/L&D/outplacement contexts).
- Strong governance, legal, and reporting requirements.
- Must show consistency and risk controls.

### Before search

What they think:
- "Can this cohort deliver measurable readiness gains under governance scrutiny?"

What they do:
- Confirm cohort scope and metric definitions before launch.
- Align with sponsor on pass/fail checkpoints.
- Train counselors on standardized cadence.

Who they reach out to:
- Client HR sponsor.
- Program operations and legal stakeholders.
- Sometimes procurement and security reviewers.

Branding techniques:
- Institution-safe language: reliability, governance, consistency.
- Evidence packets over personality-led positioning.

Narrative methods:
- Role-based narrative cards for common executive transitions.
- Red-team review for legal/compliance sensitivity in language.

### During search

Session mechanics:
- Before:
  - Review participant risk queue (stall flags, low action rates).
- During:
  - Focus on movement quality and decision confidence.
- After:
  - Structured note entry aligned to metric dictionary.
- Between:
  - Program-level intervention triggers and escalations.

Recorded data:
- Activation status.
- Signal-driven action logs.
- Session strategic yield estimate.
- Counselor intervention counts.

Measured outcomes:
- Cohort activation rate.
- Stall index.
- Session strategy-time ratio.
- Day-30 and day-60 readiness lift.

### After search

- Sponsor reporting pack with caveated claims.
- Expansion or close decision based on explicit thresholds.

## 5.4 Search-Affiliate Transition Coach

### Profile

- Often tied to retained search processes.
- Shorter, intense windows around interviews/offers.
- Must integrate with recruiter dynamics without conflict.

### Before search

- Usually enters when search is already plausible or active.
- Runs pre-briefing on mandate fit and story risk.

### During search

- High cadence prep around each interview stage.
- Narrative compression: concise, differentiated, board-safe messaging.
- Stakeholder-specific rehearsal (partner, client CEO, board member).

Session workflow:
- Before: review upcoming interview panel and mandate context.
- During: simulate likely objections and strategic pivots.
- After: debrief and adjust story architecture.
- Between: rapid-response support on new interview developments.

Metrics:
- Interview conversion step-to-step.
- Objection repeat frequency.
- Offer quality vs. target criteria.

### After search

- Offer decision coaching.
- First 90-day narrative alignment with new leadership context.

## 5.5 Board Positioning and Governance Coach

### Profile

- Long-horizon advisor for board-track transitions.
- Relationship and reputation continuity over 1-3+ years.

### Before search

- Focus on board-readiness proof and governance story.
- Build long-range relationship strategy with directors and sponsors.

### During active board search moments

- Tightens board narrative and governance thesis.
- Coaches on committee-fit language and risk posture.

Session operations:
- Before: map board composition and inflection signals.
- During: sharpen board-value narrative and political calibration.
- After: assign relationship and visibility actions.
- Between: cadence maintenance to prevent relationship dormancy.

Metrics:
- Board-relevant relationship momentum.
- Governance narrative consistency across interactions.
- Time between meaningful board-track touchpoints.

### After placement

- Ongoing board effectiveness and reputation compounding advisory.

## 6) Deep Personas: Outplacement Roles

## 6.1 Practice Leader (Outplacement P&L Owner)

### Profile

- Owns service line economics, placement outcomes, and partner renewals.
- Cares about scalability, differentiation, and margin protection.

### Before search (program design phase)

What they think:
- "Can we improve cohort outcomes without adding counselor labor cost?"

What they do:
- Define pilot scope, target cohorts, and commercial model.
- Align internal teams on operating standards and evidence expectations.

Who they reach out to:
- Client HR buyers and sponsors.
- Internal operations leadership.
- Procurement/legal as needed for larger clients.

Branding techniques:
- Firm-level promise: consistency, quality, and risk-aware delivery.
- Outcome storytelling with strict claim discipline.

Narrative design for clients:
- Program-level narrative: "structured momentum + measurable readiness." 

### During search programs

Session role (indirect):
- Oversees counselor quality and intervention discipline.
- Reviews cohort dashboards and risk exceptions.

Before/after/in-between:
- Before weekly ops review: inspect activation and stall trends.
- During review: assign interventions and owner accountability.
- After review: communicate sponsor-safe updates.
- Between: monitor threshold breaches and escalate quickly.

Records:
- Cohort scorecards.
- Intervention completion logs.
- Sponsor update memos.

Measures:
- Placement velocity and quality indicators.
- Counselor efficiency trends.
- Program NPS/CSAT and renewal risk.

### After search program cycle

- Decide expand/hold/close by cohort evidence.
- Convert outcomes into sales enablement assets.

## 6.2 Program Operations Director

### Profile

- Converts strategy into repeatable process.
- Owns launch reliability and cross-team handoffs.

### Before search

- Establishes workflow architecture: intake, cadence, escalation rules.
- Defines metric dictionary and reporting cadence.

Who they contact:
- Counselor leads.
- Analytics owner.
- Client ops counterparts.

Branding techniques:
- Operational credibility: clean handoffs, predictable cadence.

Narrative support:
- Ensures every participant gets consistent messaging templates.

### During search

Session role (meta-level):
- Ensures counselors have the right pre-session intelligence.
- Monitors compliance with session documentation standards.

Before/during/after/in-between:
- Before: preflight checks for active cohorts.
- During week: monitor exception queues.
- After: produce weekly operating packet.
- Between: refine workflows from observed friction.

Records:
- SLA adherence.
- Data completeness.
- Intervention timeliness.

Measures:
- Workflow reliability.
- Reporting latency.
- Adoption variance between counselors.

### After cycle

- Process retrospective and playbook updates.

## 6.3 Counselor Lead

### Profile

- Owns counselor coaching quality and consistency.
- Bridges operations metrics to client-facing behavior.

### Before search

- Calibrates counselor team on scripts, thresholds, and narrative standards.

### During search

Session behavior:
- Before participant sessions: reviews what-changed summaries and risk flags.
- During internal counselor sessions: trains on strategic yield, not recap.
- After: audits notes quality and intervention follow-through.
- In-between: provides quick coaching to underperforming counselors.

Records:
- Counselor quality observations.
- Session-yield estimates.
- Repeat blocker patterns.

Measures:
- Counselor variance in outcomes.
- Session strategic yield trend.
- Intervention success rates.

### After cycle

- Certifies counselor readiness for next cohort iteration.

## 6.4 Counselor / Transition Advisor

### Profile

- Front-line operator working directly with displaced executives.
- Highest emotional labor intensity.

### Before search

What they think:
- "This client is overwhelmed; I need to restore agency before velocity."

What they do:
- Stabilize emotional state and establish first 2-week rhythm.
- Build target map and outreach sequence.
- Introduce narrative backbone and confidence-building exercises.

Who they reach out to:
- Mostly client-facing only, occasionally internal specialists.

Branding techniques:
- Helps client project confidence and relevance quickly.
- Personal brand reset: headline, positioning statement, proof points.

Narrative structure:
- Situation -> mandate -> measurable outcomes -> leadership style -> next mandate fit.

### During search

Session mechanics:
- Before:
  - Review activity movement, missed commitments, and emotional signals.
- During:
  - Confidence reset + strategic prioritization + rehearsal.
- After:
  - Action commitments and schedule lock.
- In-between:
  - Nudges, reminders, and early interventions for drift.

Records:
- Session notes with blocker taxonomy.
- Outreach and response activity.
- Narrative revisions.

Measures:
- Weekly meaningful actions.
- Conversation conversion progression.
- Participant confidence and momentum trend.

### After landing

- Onboarding support and confidence transfer to new role.
- Capture lessons and testimonial opportunities.

## 6.5 Procurement and Legal Reviewer (Outplacement Context)

### Profile

- Decision gatekeeper for risk, claims, and contract boundaries.

### Before search program approval

What they think:
- "Can we defend this program commercially and legally if outcomes vary?"

What they do:
- Validate claim language, data boundaries, retention/deletion terms.
- Enforce pilot scope and pass/fail governance gates.

Who they contact:
- Practice leader, operations, security/compliance points of contact.

What they record/measure:
- Diligence completion status.
- Contract clause exceptions.
- Risk register items and mitigations.

### During program

- Minimal session role; oversight through milestone reporting.

### After program

- Approval for expansion, renewal, or close-out based on evidence and risk posture.

## 7) Job Search Timeline by Phase: Before, During, After

## 7.1 Before search (optionality)

Typical triggers to begin:
- Leadership sponsor change.
- Compensation/stagnation mismatch.
- Mandate erosion or political realignment.
- Restructure, M&A rumor, or board pressure.
- Repeated career dissonance signals (purpose, scope, influence).

Advisor priorities:
- Build narrative before urgency.
- Start quiet relationship warming.
- Define what "good role" means before inbound noise starts.
- Establish evidence cadence and confidence baseline.

## 7.2 During active search

Advisor priorities:
- Protect decision quality under emotional pressure.
- Maintain weekly execution rhythm.
- Keep narrative consistent by audience.
- Prioritize quality conversations over volume activity.

## 7.3 After landing

Advisor priorities:
- Transfer search narrative into onboarding narrative.
- Prevent buyer's remorse and early confidence dips.
- Capture proof and referrals.
- Preserve optionality system for future transitions.

## 9) Branding and Narrative Architecture

## 9.1 Advisor brand techniques

Coaches:
- Publish role-specific POV content.
- Use anonymized case patterns with measured claims.
- Emphasize strategic leverage over generic motivation.

Outplacement firms:
- Emphasize cohort reliability and governance rigor.
- Position as low-risk transition partner.
- Use board-safe proof language.

## 9.2 Executive client brand techniques

- Reframe profile from responsibilities to enterprise outcomes.
- Build "mandate fit" signal in summary language.
- Maintain one master narrative with audience-specific expression.
- Use relationship-led visibility, not random broad posting.

## 9.3 Narrative template (applies across roles)

1. Legacy proof
- "What I inherited and what changed under my leadership."

2. Inflection and timing
- "Why I am moving now and what context shifted."

3. Future mandate fit
- "Where my operating edge is strongest next."

4. Risk and objection handling
- "Why this is not a repeat of common failure modes."

5. Decision anchor
- "What role conditions are non-negotiable."

## 10) Session Operating Model by Persona (Standardized Checklist)

Every high-performing coach/outplacement session follows the same control loop:

Before:
- Review movement since last touch.
- Identify top 1-3 strategic decisions needed today.
- Pre-load likely objections and confidence risks.

During:
- Open with emotional-state calibration.
- Move quickly to strategic decisions and narrative rehearsal.
- Confirm explicit next actions with owner and deadline.

After:
- Write concise decision log.
- Update risk flags and intervention triggers.

In-between:
- Monitor adherence.
- Trigger intervention if action threshold missed.

Recorded fields (minimum):
- Date, session type, participant state.
- Strategic decisions made.
- Actions committed and due dates.
- Risks and blockers.
- Narrative version changes.

Measured fields (minimum):
- Strategy-time ratio.
- Action completion rate.
- Signal-to-action latency.
- Conversation progression velocity.
- Confidence/momentum trend.
