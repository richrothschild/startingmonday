# The Offer Negotiation Guide

startingmonday.app | May 2026

---

The offer conversation is the one conversation in the search that candidates at every level routinely handle worse than they should. Not because negotiation is difficult — it is not — but because by the time the offer arrives, the candidate is tired, relieved, and afraid that saying anything might cost them the opportunity. So they say nothing, or they ask for something modest, or they accept the first number.

This costs real money. A CIO who accepts the first offer at $500,000 when the company had room to $550,000 has made a $50,000 mistake in a single conversation. That conversation took five minutes. This guide is about having it correctly.

---

## Before the Offer Arrives

The negotiation begins well before the offer letter. It begins when you decide what you need and what you will walk away from, before you are sitting in front of a number you are being asked to accept.

Know your target, your floor, and what you would trade compensation for. The target is the total compensation you are aiming for at this level in this market, including base, bonus structure, equity, and benefits. The floor is the number below which you will not go, regardless of other factors. The trades are the things you value enough that you would accept a lower base for — more equity, better title, geographic flexibility, an accelerated review, a more interesting mandate.

Write these down before any conversations go deep. Do not make these decisions with an offer on the table and a 48-hour deadline.

---

## When the Offer Arrives

Thank them. Mean it. Then ask for time to review it carefully. Twenty-four to forty-eight hours is appropriate for an executive-level offer. If they press for an immediate answer, that tells you something about how they operate. A company that cannot give a CIO-level hire 48 hours to review a compensation package is not one you want to start a relationship with by capitulating.

Do not negotiate the first number out loud in the moment. "Can I get back to you by [specific date]?" is the correct response. Write down the full offer — base, bonus target and structure, equity grant and vesting schedule, benefits, start date, title — before you evaluate any part of it. Evaluating a compensation package on incomplete information is how candidates make bad decisions.

---

## The Negotiation Conversation

Choose one or two things to negotiate. Not everything. Attempting to negotiate every element of an offer simultaneously signals desperation or poor judgment, and it rarely produces better outcomes than focused, specific asks.

The highest-value conversation in executive compensation is often not about the base salary — it is about the bonus structure and the equity. A base that is $25,000 below your target matters less if the equity structure is strong and the bonus is paid on metrics you can influence. Understand what you are actually evaluating before you decide what to push on.

When you make the ask, anchor it to something real. Market data, your current compensation, the scope of the role, or a specific value you bring that you believe the offer does not reflect — these are all legitimate anchors. "I was hoping for something closer to $550,000 given my experience with PE-backed integration mandates and the scope of what we discussed" is a complete negotiation opening. It names the number, provides a reason, and does not apologize.

Do not threaten and do not bluff. "I have another offer at X" when you do not is a bad bet — if they call it, you have no move. If you do have another offer, you do not need to exaggerate it. "I have a competing offer I am considering" is sufficient and true.

Silence is a tool. When you make an ask, wait. The first person to speak after an ask often concedes ground. Do not concede your own ask by filling the silence with qualifications.

## What Is Usually Negotiable

Base salary is typically negotiable within a range the company has established before the offer goes out. That range is usually 10 to 20 percent above the initial offer at the executive level, though it varies significantly by company and by how urgently they need the role filled.

Sign-on bonus is often more flexible than base salary because it does not compound into future raises, does not affect the salary structure, and is classified differently on the company's books. If you are walking away from unvested equity at your current employer, a sign-on bonus that offsets that loss is a standard and expected ask.

Equity vesting is negotiable, particularly the cliff. A four-year vest with a one-year cliff is standard. A three-year vest, an accelerated vest on termination without cause, or double-trigger acceleration in a change of control are all worth asking about.

Start date is almost always flexible within four to six weeks of the proposed date.

Title is sometimes negotiable, particularly the distinction between SVP, EVP, and C-suite at companies where those distinctions matter for how the role reports.

What is usually not negotiable once an offer is made: the reporting structure, the scope of the role, or the core mandate. If you have concerns about those, raise them before the offer, not in the negotiation.

---

## The Graceful Walk-Away

If the offer does not reach your floor after a good-faith negotiation, decline it without burning the relationship. "I have a lot of respect for the team and the opportunity, but I'm not able to make the numbers work given what I'm leaving and what the market looks like for this scope. I hope we can stay in touch." That is the complete message.

The executive search community is small. The hiring manager who could not make the offer work today is the operating partner who places you in their next portfolio company in two years. Do not make the walk-away a conversation they remember badly.

---

## After You Accept

Send a brief note to your primary contact confirming your acceptance and expressing genuine enthusiasm for the role. Brief is better. The effusive multi-paragraph email of gratitude is uncomfortable to receive and immediately forgettable.

Start reading before you start. Review the information they provide during the pre-start period. Ask for introductions to the two or three people you most need to understand before day one. Arrive having already done the work of knowing what you are walking into.

The prep brief for your new employer is worth generating in the week before you start. You will see the company differently once you are inside. But you will also arrive with context the brief surfaces that you would not have thought to look for on your own.
