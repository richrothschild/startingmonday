# Writing Guidelines — Resumes, Cover Letters, Messaging

Apply these to every piece of outbound writing. AI-generated copy fails because it overuses a predictable set of rhetorical tricks. Avoid them.

---

## Punctuation Rules

**No em dashes. Ever.**
Em dashes (—) are the single most reliable signal of AI-generated text. Remove every one. Replace with:
- A period (split into two sentences — usually the right fix)
- A comma (if the clause is genuinely subordinate)
- Parentheses (if the aside is truly parenthetical)
- Or restructure the sentence so the aside isn't needed

This applies to all outreach messages, resumes, cover letters, briefings, and any other written output.

---

## Patterns to Eliminate

**Antithesis ("It's not X. It's Y.")**
Signals the opposite of original thinking. Find a direct statement instead.

**Bathos**
Builds to a climax, lands on nothing. "After years of searching, the answer was simple: just show up." Every sentence should earn its landing.

**Chiasmus**
"Not working harder — working smarter. Not smarter — harder." Sounds clever, says nothing twice. Cut it.

**Dramatic pause before a non-revelation**
"The real problem isn't the market — it's you." If the reveal isn't actually revealing, remove the setup.

**Epistrophe creep**
Ending three consecutive sentences with the same word for emphasis. Once is a choice. Three times is a tic.

**The false binary**
"You're either building or you're decaying." Collapses nuance into drama. Usually wrong. Readers notice.

**False scarcity pivot ("Most people do X. The best ones do Y.")**
Positions the reader as exceptional for reading a LinkedIn post. Recruiters and executives see through it instantly.

**Grandiose close**
"This isn't just a strategy. It's a reckoning." A practical point doesn't need to feel historic. Let results speak.

**The humility flex**
"I used to think I had all the answers. I was wrong." Positions failure as wisdom, wisdom as brand. Authentic failure is specific — name it or cut it.

**Noun stacking**
"A framework for scalable, sustainable, human-centred impact-driven growth." Words that mean everything mean nothing. One noun, one modifier.

**The obvious reframe**
"It's not about what you make. It's about why you make it." Taking a known idea, italicizing one word, calling it a new way of seeing. Not new.

**Parasocial warmth**
"I see you. I know this is hard." Intimacy at scale, manufactured. Executives find this condescending.

**Pseudo-logic ("The ocean is vast. Therefore your brand needs a clear voice.")**
Unrelated observation followed by "therefore" to imply logic that isn't there. Every claim needs a real connection.

**Sentences shaped like insight but empty on arrival**
"The real product was the people all along." Cut on sight.

**The rhetorical ladder**
Stacks three questions before answering any of them. Momentum without substance. Answer first, then explain.

---

## What Works Instead

- Specific numbers over sweeping claims ("KPI attainment from 62% to 93%" not "dramatically improved performance")
- One concrete outcome per claim
- Direct subject-verb-object sentences
- Name the company's actual problem, not a generic version of it
- Creative direction = knowing what *not* to say, and how to say what remains
