# The Executive Search Playbook

startingmonday.app | May 2026

---

Most executive searches fail the same way. Not because the candidate lacks the credentials. Not because the market is soft. They fail because the candidate approaches a campaign like a job hunt — posting a resume, telling their network, waiting for inbound — and then wonders why six months later nothing has materialized at the right level.

This is the playbook for running a search that works. It is direct and specific. Apply it from day one and you will finish faster, position better, and walk into every conversation prepared.

---

## What an Executive Search Actually Is

At VP and above, the best roles are not posted. They are filled through a combination of relationships, timing, and positioning that happens weeks or months before a job description appears anywhere public. The candidate who learns about a role the day it posts on LinkedIn is already behind three people who knew it was coming.

This means the search is not an application process. It is a campaign. You identify target organizations, monitor them for signals, build relationships before you need them, and position yourself so that when the moment arrives — a new mandate, a departing leader, a portfolio company in transition — you are already in conversation with the right people.

The rest of this document is about how to run that campaign.

---

## Phase 1: Define What You Are Looking For

The most common mistake in an executive search is starting before this question is answered. Not answered vaguely — answered specifically.

Before you add a single company to your list, write down: the mandate type you want (transformation, growth, integration, steady-state), the scale that fits your record (company revenue, team size, PE-backed versus public versus private), the sectors where your experience is directly translatable, and the geography you will actually consider. This takes an hour. Do it on paper, not in your head.

Your positioning summary follows from this. It is not a list of accomplishments — your resume carries those. It is a one-paragraph answer to the question a CEO or operating partner will ask first: what specifically do you do, and what kind of situation calls for you? The candidates who struggle to answer this clearly in conversation never fixed it in writing first. Do the writing.

---

## Phase 2: Build the Target List

Twenty to thirty companies is the right size for an executive search target list. Not two hundred. Not eight. Twenty to thirty.

Two hundred is a job seeker's list. It signals no selectivity and produces no depth. Eight is too narrow — you will have gaps between conversations and no buffer for companies that move slowly or not at all.

Build your list by starting with the organizations where you have the highest conviction: companies in your sector, at the right scale, with a mandate type that matches your record. Then add the ones where you have a relationship — a contact at the company, a mutual connection, a history that gives you a credible reason to reach out. Finally, add a small set of aspirational targets — companies where the fit is strong but the path is less clear.

For each company on the list, record the career page URL, the pipeline stage (most will start at Watching), and your fit assessment on a one-to-ten scale. These numbers matter because they help you prioritize your time as the list generates activity.

## Phase 3: Watch Before You Move

The scan happens while you are doing other things. This is the part of the search most candidates skip entirely because it does not feel like doing something, and executive candidates in search feel compelled to do something constantly.

The discipline of watching is this: set up monitoring on your target companies and let the system surface role activity and intelligence signals before you act. A signal fires — a new CFO hire at a company you have been watching — and you reach out to your contact there that day. A career page scan detects a newly created role that fits your background before it appears anywhere else — you are in the first conversation before the market knows the role exists.

The candidates who are always early to conversations did not get there by being faster. They got there by watching sooner.

---

## Phase 4: Build Relationships Before You Need Them

Every contact in your target network falls into one of three categories: people who can refer you, people who can advocate for you, and people who will be the decision-maker. The third category is the hardest to reach and the most important to cultivate. The way you reach them is through the first two.

Map your contacts against your target list. For each company on the list, identify who you know at the company, who you know who knows someone at the company, and who you know who has worked with the company's leadership. These are your first moves.

Outreach works when it is specific and not obviously self-interested. "I saw you recently joined the board at Acme Corp — I've been watching that company for six months and would love your read on what they're building" is a real conversation. "I'm exploring opportunities and would appreciate any introductions" is not.

Follow-up is where most searches die. A conversation happens. The contact says they will think of you. You wait two weeks, feel awkward, and do not follow up. The contact has moved on. Set a follow-up reminder within 48 hours of every conversation. Not a week. Forty-eight hours.

---

## Phase 5: Prepare Before Every Conversation

Walking into a first call without full preparation at the executive level is a recoverable mistake once. It is not recoverable twice, and the executive search market is small enough that word travels.

Full preparation means knowing the company's recent history, current priorities, leadership team, and the specific challenges that make your background relevant to them. It means knowing what questions to expect and having answers to each that draw on your specific record, not generic leadership principles. It means walking in with a perspective — something you noticed about their market or their situation that demonstrates you have been paying attention.

This preparation takes four hours to do manually. The prep brief generates it in sixty seconds and goes deeper than most manual research because it draws on sources most candidates do not have time to pull together. Use it before every first call. Use it before every second call too, because the brief updates as new intelligence surfaces.

---

## Phase 6: Manage the Pipeline Like a Project

A search that is not tracked is a search that is losing ground. You have twenty companies at various stages, forty contacts with conversations in progress, follow-ups due this week and last week, and signals firing at three of your target companies simultaneously. Without a system, you will miss something. At this level, missing something has consequences.

Track every company by stage. Log every conversation with a note and a follow-up date. When a signal fires, record what you did in response. When a role is detected at a target company, move the company up in priority and note the date. The audit trail matters because searches run for months and memory degrades. The note you write today about a conversation is the context for the call you take in six weeks.

Review the pipeline every Monday. Ten minutes. What moved? What needs attention? What has gone quiet that should not have?

---

## The Search in Numbers

Executives who run structured searches — defined target list, consistent monitoring, disciplined follow-up — reach the offer stage in four to seven months at the CIO/CTO/CFO level. The unstructured search, driven by inbound and intermittent effort, takes nine to fourteen months for the same candidate profile. The difference is not luck. It is system.

The role you want may already be forming at one of your target companies. The question is whether you will know before anyone else does.
