# Search Firm Landing Page Council Review
**Synthetic Expert Council Assessment**  
May 15, 2026 | Reviewers: Chris Voss, Robert Cialdini, Mark Horstman

---

## Executive Consensus

The revised search-firm page is directionally right. It now speaks more clearly to the firm's economics: less rework, better kickoff quality, tighter shortlist velocity. That is the correct market.

The remaining issue is that the page still reads a little too much like a product description and not enough like a retained-search business case. The firm needs to see three things immediately:

1. **What does this do for my practice?**
2. **Why should I trust it enough to put it in front of a partner or consultant?**
3. **How does it reduce friction in the actual search workflow?**

The page is close, but it should be more explicit about partner time saved, client confidence gained, and mandate wins supported.

---

## Chris Voss — Communication & Objection Perspective

### What works

- The hero now says what the buyer gets: better kickoff quality, sharper candidate positioning, faster mandate-to-shortlist movement.
- The page removes software jargon and avoids forcing a workflow change.
- The CTA to review a sample CFO brief is concrete and low-risk.

### What fails

1. **The page still assumes the buyer already agrees with the premise.**
   - A search firm partner may think: “We already do research. Why do we need this?”
   - That objection is not surfaced or answered early enough.

2. **The page explains the service before acknowledging the buyer's pressure.**
   - Voss would push you to label the real constraint first: limited partner time, inconsistent consultant prep, and pressure to look sharper in client kickoffs.

3. **The trust objection is not handled explicitly.**
   - Search firms are careful about anything that could feel like generic content or a thin wrapper around research.
   - The page needs a stronger line about what is unique, what is customized, and what is not being replaced.

### What Voss would change

- Add a short objection-led line under the hero:
  - “If your team already researches every mandate, this is not a replacement. It is the briefing layer that keeps partners from starting from zero.”
- Add a “what this is not” block:
  - Not a CRM.
  - Not a generic intelligence feed.
  - Not another dashboard for consultants to ignore.
- Replace one of the current benefit statements with a calibrated question:
  - “How much time does your team lose every week re-briefing the same search from scratch?”

### Voss Grade: **B+**

**What gets it to A:** Lead with the buyer's hidden objection, then answer it in one sentence before describing the product.

## Robert Cialdini — Ethical Persuasion Perspective

### What works

- The page now has more authority because it is framed around firm outcomes, not generic productivity.
- The sample brief CTA is a strong commitment step.
- The “pilot this on your next two retained mandates” ask is a good low-friction commitment path.

### What fails

1. **There is not enough proof density.**
   - Cialdini would say the page has claims, but not enough visible evidence before the ask.
   - A buyer should see at least one named or well-anchored proof point, even if sanitized.

2. **Authority is implied, not established.**
   - The page says “for retained search firms,” but it does not show why the firm should regard Starting Monday as credible in that world.
   - The buyer needs a signal that this understands retained search mechanics, not just outreach language.

3. **Commitment is present, but reciprocity is weak.**
   - You ask for a pilot, but you do not yet give a sufficiently compelling sample artifact or benchmark result to make the exchange feel obviously fair.

### What Cialdini would change

- Add one visible proof block near the top:
  - “Example output: a CFO brief with market context, candidate framing, and interview filters in one page.”
- Add authority cues:
  - Mention retained-search workflow language the buyer already uses: mandate, kickoff, slate, shortlist, candidate prep.
- Add a stronger reciprocity signal:
  - “Review one real brief first, then decide whether to pilot it.”

### Cialdini Grade: **A-**

**What gets it to A:** Add concrete proof and authority cues before the main CTA so the ask feels earned.

## Mark Horstman — Execution Discipline Perspective

### What works

- The page is more disciplined than before.
- It now speaks in operational terms: prep time, shortlist quality, cycle speed.
- The process steps are clean and easy to scan.

### What fails

1. **The outcome language is still slightly fuzzy.**
   - Horstman would want the page to define the operating rhythm more precisely.
   - “Improve kickoff quality” is good, but what does the consultant actually do differently on Monday morning?

2. **The page does not make the cadence visible.**
   - Search firms run on repeatable workflows.
   - The page should show how this fits into the firm's weekly rhythm: mandate intake, kickoff prep, candidate prep, shortlist review.

3. **Measurement is suggested, not operationalized.**
   - The page says users can track improvements, but does not define the exact metric set.
   - Horstman would insist on a simple scoreboard.

### What Horstman would change

- Add a short operating cadence block:
  - Monday: mandate intake.
  - Tuesday: brief delivery.
  - Wednesday: consultant prep.
  - Thursday: candidate framing.
  - Friday: shortlist review.
- Add one KPI row:
  - Consultant prep hours saved.
  - First-slate acceptance rate.
  - Mid-search resets avoided.
- Tighten one line so it sounds more like execution than marketing:
  - “Your team gets a briefing artifact they can use before kickoff, not another tool they have to manage.”

### Horstman Grade: **A-**

**What gets it to A:** Make the operating cadence and success metrics explicit enough that a partner could run the process without interpretation.

---

## Consolidated Recommendations

1. **Open with the firm's pain, not the product.**
   - Re-briefing, inconsistent prep, and lost partner time should be named early.

2. **Add one proof artifact above the fold or immediately below it.**
   - A sample brief image, excerpt, or KPI panel would materially improve trust.

3. **Make the cadence visible.**
   - Show how this fits retained-search workflow week by week.

4. **Keep the ask small.**
   - Review a sample brief, then pilot two mandates.

5. **Spell out the trust boundary.**
   - Be explicit that this complements retained search research rather than replacing it.
