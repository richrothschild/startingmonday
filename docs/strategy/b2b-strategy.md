# B2B Strategy

> Defining the buyer, the sales motion, and the build sequence for Starting Monday's B2B channel.

---

## Three buyer types

There is no single B2B buyer. The opportunity spans three types, each with different deal mechanics, decision speed, and product requirements.

### Type 1: Boutique executive coach (highest priority)

An independent coach with 10-30 active executive clients at any time. They specialize in senior transitions — often former HR executives, former search partners, or experienced coaches with a CIO, CISO, or CTO niche. They make every decision themselves. No procurement process.

**What they want:** A tool that makes their clients more organized and active, that reflects well on the coach, and that does not create administrative overhead. They are not looking to resell software. They want to be the expert who knows what works.

**Deal mechanic:** Referral or bundle. Either the coach recommends it and the client pays directly (coach gets referral credit), or the coach includes it in their monthly engagement fee and pays the seat cost themselves. Both work.

### Type 2: Boutique outplacement firm

A firm with 5-20 consultants serving 50-200 clients per year at executive level. Think a regional firm that handles executive transitions for companies going through restructuring. Decision-maker is the managing director or owner.

**What they want:** To differentiate their service offering, to have something to show clients beyond workshops and resume critiques, and to demonstrate ROI. They are competing against Right Management and Lee Hecht Harrison and their differentiator is quality and personalization.

**Deal mechanic:** Seat license. They want to buy X seats per month and assign them to active clients. They need a dashboard to see who is using it. They will want a co-brand or white-label option within 6 months of onboarding.

### Type 3: Enterprise HR / severance programs

A large company going through a layoff that wants to offer career transition support as part of the package. Decision-maker is the CHRO or VP HR.

**Deal mechanic:** Bulk license per departing employee. Could be a one-time deal or a standing agreement.

This buyer is real but has a 6-18 month sales cycle, legal review, and security questionnaires. It is not a year-1 target.

## What product is needed for each buyer

| Buyer | Product requirement | Timeline |
|-------|-------------------|----------|
| Coach (referral) | Referral code + attribution tracking | Sprint 4 |
| Coach (bundle) | Partner dashboard, client list, activity summary | Sprint 4-5 |
| Outplacement firm | Seat purchase, client assignment, co-brand | Sprint 5-6 |
| Enterprise HR | SSO, data isolation, SOC 2, legal agreements | Year 2 |

Do not build seat management before you have 3-5 paid pilot referral partners. You will build the wrong thing.

## The sales sequence

**Step 1 — 30 days: Build the case study base.**
Individual users are already signing up. Every one of them is a future case study. Get permission to quote results. A testimonial from a CIO who landed in 90 days while using the platform is worth more than any brochure.

**Step 2 — 60 days: Identify 10 executive coaches.**
LinkedIn search: "executive coach" + "CIO" or "VP Technology" + "career transition." Filter for ICF-certified coaches with a visible online presence and clearly senior client base. Target coaches who post about career search topics — they are already trying to build credibility in this space.

Outreach message: "I built a search management platform for senior technology executives. I have [N] users in active search right now. I would like to offer you 3 free seats for 90 days for your clients. If it works, we can talk about a referral arrangement. No commitment."

**Step 3 — 90 days: Run the pilot.**
Three coaches, three clients each. Check in at 30 and 60 days. Document what the coaches say to their clients about how to use the platform. That language becomes your positioning and your onboarding copy.

**Step 4 — 120 days: Structure the offer.**
Pilots that work convert to a referral deal: coach gets 20% of subscriber revenue they generate, paid monthly. Or: coach pays $79 per seat per month, charges clients at their discretion. Let coaches choose which model fits their business.

## Referral revenue model

At 20% commission on Search ($199/mo): $39.80 per subscriber per month.

A coach with 10 active clients: $398/month in recurring referral income.

At 10 coaches each with 10 clients: 100 subscribers from the partner channel. At $199/month blended, that is $19,900 MRR. Your cost: $3,980 in referral commissions. Net: $15,920 MRR with ~80% gross margin on the partner channel.

This is the highest-margin acquisition channel in the model.

## The Manager Tools opportunity

Mark Horstman is not just a demo target. He is potentially the highest-value channel partner in this market. If he genuinely believes in the product, a mention on the Manager Tools podcast or newsletter reaches hundreds of thousands of managers and executives. Many of them are either in search or know someone who is.

The pitch to him is not "promote my product." The pitch is: "Your listeners ask you constantly about job search tools for senior executives. I built the one I could not find when I was in search. I would like you to use it, break it, and tell me honestly whether it is worth recommending." That is a product review request, not a sales call. If he likes it, the endorsement comes naturally.

---

## Why not go straight to enterprise HR

Two reasons. First, the sales cycle is 6-18 months and requires features that do not yet exist. Second, enterprise HR buyers purchase outplacement as a commodity. The decision is made on price and brand recognition, not product quality. Right Management has 50 years of brand. That is not a fight to pick in year 1.

The path to enterprise: 500 individual users → 20 coach partners → 3-5 outplacement firms → 1-2 enterprise references → enterprise HR conversations backed by proof.
