Date: 2026-05-26
Scope: Outreach email rewrites using council members as role-specific drafting lenses

Goal:
- Speed up rewrites by assigning each council member to the email component where they add the most value.
- Capture specific member feedback on what to change now.

Email components used in this matrix:
- Subject line
- First sentence
- Objection handling
- Proof line
- CTA

## Specific Feedback by Email Section

### Subject Line
Current issue:
- Some lines still read operational or generic, especially in cross-channel variants.

Council-directed changes:
1. Use one explicit tension per segment: readiness, momentum, or decision-quality risk.
2. Add role anchor in every subject line for executive-grade specificity.
3. Avoid product-first nouns in subject lines unless a proof asset is named.

Examples to prefer:
- "Quick question on CIO transition readiness at Northstar Health"
- "Quick question on mandate quality risk for Partner searches"

Examples to avoid:
- "Quick question" alone without role or context
- Generic "platform" and "workflow" language in the subject

### First Sentence
Current issue:
- Better than before, but still occasionally framework-heavy and less human than needed.

Council-directed changes:
1. Start with situational truth the recipient recognizes now.
2. Include one emotional-state cue only when relevant; avoid stacking multiple states.
3. Keep opening sentence to one idea and one consequence.

Examples to prefer:
- "CIO transitions usually stall in week one when narrative quality lags signal quality."
- "Board-track candidates often look qualified, then lose momentum in the first governance conversation."

### Objection Handling
Current issue:
- Some branches are still generic and not segment-economic.

Council-directed changes:
1. Translate objections into operating or economic consequences.
2. Acknowledge concern first, then offer one reversible next step.
3. Remove defensive language and avoid over-explaining.

Examples to prefer:
- "Fair concern. If this does not reduce partner prep rework in 30 days, stop."
- "Fair point. If it does not improve first-conversation readiness, pass."

### Proof Line
Current issue:
- Stronger now, but proof phrasing still varies too much by channel.

Council-directed changes:
1. Keep one canonical legal-safe proof structure across channels.
2. Include denominator and caveat every time.
3. Add channel-specific context after canonical proof, not before.

Canonical pattern:
- "In our Jan-May 2026 pilot cohort (n=27), median time to first qualified outreach was 9 days; results vary by market conditions and execution quality."

### CTA
Current issue:
- Major improvement with yes/pass, but some lines could be shorter and more natural.

Council-directed changes:
1. Keep binary yes/pass in every template and follow-up.
2. Mention exactly one asset in CTA.
3. Remove extra option branches from first touch.

Preferred CTA:
- "If you want it, reply yes and I will send the [asset]. If not useful now, reply pass and I will close the loop."

## Consolidated Findings

Highest-confidence findings:
1. The best-performing rewrite workflow is role-specific first sentence + canonical proof line + binary yes/pass CTA.
2. Emotional-state framing works best in sentence 1 only; repeating state language later reduces natural tone.
3. Search-firm and outplacement emails need stronger economics and operating-model language than executive and coach emails.
4. Legal-safe proof quality is now a competitive advantage and should not be diluted for conversational tone.
5. Human tone improves when component labels are removed, while structure stays intact in drafting logic.
