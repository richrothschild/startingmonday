# Mark Horstman Product Review

> A pre-demo analysis of Starting Monday through Mark Horstman's lens.
> Mark co-hosts Manager Tools, the #1 management podcast. His worldview: behavior, not information, determines outcomes. Every critique he makes flows from that thesis.

---

## The premise

Mark has spent two decades coaching managers on one thesis: if you want different results, change what you do, not what you know. He is skeptical of any tool that helps people feel productive without changing their behavior. He will apply that standard to Starting Monday immediately.

---

## What holds up under his scrutiny

**The follow-up system.** The strongest feature in the product by his standards. Scheduled, non-optional touchpoints with specific people is exactly what he would call the right behavior. He would recognize it as the professional equivalent of the O3 (scheduled one-on-one). The "Actions Due" counter in the daily briefing is the metric that actually matters to him.

**Signals as outreach triggers, not just information.** The design intent — that a funding round or exec departure prompts a specific outreach angle — is sound. He would distinguish this from a news aggregator. The `angle` field in signal alerts is doing real work: it tells the user why to reach out, not just what happened.

**The executive framing.** Manager Tools' audience skews senior. He would appreciate that this product is not built for entry-level spray-and-pray. The tone of the copy ("Your search is live," not "Welcome to your journey") and the role-specific signal framing (CIO, CISO, COO with tailored signal focus) are things he would recognize as done deliberately.

**The daily briefing as habit architecture.** He understands the logic of a morning ritual that creates a consistent scan cycle. His whole career is built on daily and weekly cadence behaviors. The briefing time selection, timezone support, and the "sent before you start work" design are aligned with how he thinks about routines.

---

## Where he would push back hard

**"What behavior does this change?"** This is his opening question. A user can read the briefing every morning for 30 days, feel very informed, and have started zero conversations. There is no metric in the product today that tracks outreach sent versus signals received. He would call this the central design gap.

**The prep brief.** He would be skeptical of AI-generated "likely objections" and "outreach angles" at scale. Not because the technology is wrong, but because false confidence is worse than no preparation. If the prep brief says "they will ask about your ERP experience" and they do not, the candidate spent 30 minutes preparing for the wrong conversation. He would ask: "What is the source on the objections? Is it trained on real interview data for this company and role, or is it pattern-matching on generic executive interview content?"

**Company-first vs. relationship-first.** The product's primary object is a company. Mark would argue the primary object should be a person. He would say: "You do not get hired by a company. You get hired by a person who decides to advocate for you." The contacts feature exists, but it is secondary to the company and signal architecture. He would push on whether this reinforces the wrong mental model for how executive placements actually happen.

**The AI briefing quality question.** The briefing runs on Haiku with a 1024-token cap. For a paying user generating it every day, output quality variance matters. He would want to see an example briefing and ask: "Is this what a senior advisor would actually tell me? Or is this what sounds like what a senior advisor would tell me?" The distinction matters enormously to him.

**The Kanban board.** He would be suspicious of a pipeline metaphor applied to a job search. He would say: "Sales pipeline metrics work because you have volume and you control the inputs. An executive search has 5 to 15 companies in play. You do not need a Kanban board for 15 rows." He would want to know what problem the visual board solves that a simple list does not.

---

## The central tension he would name

The product is built on an intelligence model: gather better information, get better results. His model is different: execute the right behaviors consistently, get better results. These are compatible but not the same thing, and the product does not currently bridge them explicitly.

He would say it is possible to build a product that is genuinely behavior-driving rather than information-providing. The follow-up reminder system proves the product can do this. But the center of gravity is still signals, matches, and intelligence — not actions, conversations, and relationships.

If he is generous, he would call this version 1. If he is not, he would say: "This helps you feel like you are working on your search. That is not the same as working on your search."

---

## Questions he would ask in a demo

1. "What is the average number of outreach messages a user sends per week? How does that compare to before they started using this?"
2. "If I add 20 companies and check my briefing every day for 30 days, how many conversations am I likely to have had?"
3. "The prep brief says 'likely objections' — where does that come from? Show me how it is different for Acme Corp versus their direct competitor."
4. "Why is the primary navigation object a company rather than a person?"
5. "What is the difference between this and setting up Google Alerts for my 20 target companies and keeping a spreadsheet of follow-ups?"

---

## How to prepare for the conversation

Answer question 5 directly before he asks it. The honest answer: the briefing synthesizes multiple signals through a role-specific lens, the prep brief contextualizes that against your specific background, and the follow-up system makes the next action non-optional rather than a note in your calendar. That is a real answer.

On behavior vs. information: do not defend the current architecture — acknowledge the gap and name what is coming. Outreach action tracking (logging that you sent the email, not just that you had the angle) is the missing link. Tell him it is being built. The sprint plan makes it Sprint 1.

On prep brief objections: be honest that quality scales with data. A well-populated company profile with a Crunchbase ID and career page link produces a sharper brief than an empty one. That puts the user in control of quality.

He will respect directness more than polish. Walk in and say "here is what it does well and here is what it does not do yet." That starts a real conversation.

---

## The value proposition that survives his scrutiny

Starting Monday gives a senior executive a daily forcing function to stay active and informed, with the infrastructure to convert information into action. The current gap is measurement — the product does not yet close the loop from signal to conversation to outcome. That is what the next sprint cycle delivers.
