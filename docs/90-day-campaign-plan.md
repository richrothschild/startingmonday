# The 90-Day Campaign Plan

startingmonday.app | May 2026

---

Most executive searches take longer than they should because they start slowly, peak somewhere in month two or three, and then lose momentum. The candidate becomes tired, the outreach becomes sporadic, and the weekly discipline erodes into checking the pipeline when something happens to remind them it exists.

This plan front-loads the work, builds sustainable habits in the middle, and finishes the search with the active momentum it needs to close. It is designed for an executive in full active search — not a passive monitor, not someone testing the waters. Someone who needs to arrive at an offer within 90 days or close to it.

---

## What 90 Days Actually Looks Like

Most roles at the VP and above level take 90 to 180 days from first conversation to offer. A 90-day campaign is not a promise that the search ends in 90 days — it is a commitment to the actions that make 90 days a realistic outcome. The candidates who arrive at offers in that window are the ones who built the right infrastructure, reached the right contacts, and prepared with enough depth to compete in the conversations that mattered.

---

## Month One: Build the Foundation

The first 30 days are for building. Not for networking broadly, not for applying to postings, but for setting up the infrastructure that makes the next 60 days productive.

Week one is setup. Add your 20 to 30 target companies to the pipeline with career page URLs. Complete your profile: upload your resume, import LinkedIn, set your briefing time. Generate your strategy brief and read it twice. Write down the two or three things in the brief that surprised you or sharpened your thinking. These are the positioning gaps you will close over the next 30 days.

Week two is positioning. Revise your positioning summary based on what the strategy brief surfaced. Write your opening summary — the 90-second version of your background you will use to open every conversation — and practice it aloud until it sounds natural. Write the two-paragraph version of your story for the executives and operating partners you will be reaching out to. The work you do here makes every outreach and every conversation more effective.

Week three is the contact audit. Review every professional relationship that might be relevant to the search and categorize them: who can refer you, who can advocate for you, and who is in a position to hire someone like you. For each contact in the first two categories, write down when you last spoke and what your opening message will be. For the third category, identify the path to a warm introduction and who can provide it.

Week four is the first wave of outreach. Send reconnection messages to your warmest contacts — the people where the conversation is easy and the relationship is current. The goal for month one is not conversations. It is foundations: infrastructure built, positioning sharp, contacts primed, and the first outreach in flight.

---

## Month Two: Generate Conversations

Month two is when the search becomes active. The infrastructure is in place. Now you fill the calendar.

The weekly outreach target is five messages per week. Not five cold messages — five total, mixing warm reactivations, referral asks, and targeted cold outreach to organizations where you have specific intelligence and a credible reason to engage. Track the response rate. If it falls below 15 to 20 percent, the messaging is wrong, not the effort level.

Every time a signal fires at a target company, treat it as an action item within 48 hours. Reach out to your contact at that company or request an introduction through a mutual connection. This is the timing advantage that monitoring produces — the candidate who reaches out the week of an executive departure is in a materially different conversation than the one who reaches out eight weeks later.

The prep brief is your preparation for every conversation during this month. Every first call gets a fresh brief generated that morning. Log every conversation within 24 hours. Set every follow-up reminder before you close the tab. The discipline of logging is what allows month three to be productive.

Track pipeline velocity: how many new conversations are starting each week, how many are progressing to second calls, how many are stalling. If new conversations are not starting at the rate you need, the problem is outreach volume or message quality. If conversations are stalling at first calls, the positioning in the call itself may need attention.

---

## Month Three: Compete and Close

Month three is for the conversations that are advancing and the new ones that the activity from month two is now generating. The search has its own momentum by this point. Your job is to sustain it and not let the search stall while you are focused on the promising threads.

Continue the weekly outreach discipline even when you have active conversations. The mistake most candidates make is stopping outreach when two or three threads look promising. Those threads often take longer than expected or do not close. The candidate who stopped outreach in week eight and then watches a promising conversation go quiet in week ten is back at zero. Keep the pipeline fed.

For conversations that are advancing toward an offer conversation, do your most thorough preparation. A second or third call is not a repeat of the first — the context has evolved and the questions are more specific. Use the prep brief and add everything you learned in the previous conversation to the company workspace. The interviewer is evaluating whether you are as prepared and specific as you were in the first call. Exceed that expectation.

By day 75, take a week to review the full 90-day picture. What moved? What did not? What was the pattern in the conversations that advanced versus the ones that stalled? This review, done honestly, produces the information you need to make the final 15 days count.

---

## What Success Looks Like at 90 Days

An offer in hand is the best outcome. But not every 90-day campaign ends with an offer — some end with two or three active conversations in late stages, which is a strong position. Some end with a clear picture of why the search has taken longer than expected and a specific adjustment to address it. All three of these are results. What is not a result is 90 days of unfocused effort that leaves no clear picture of what happened or why.

Run the campaign with discipline and specificity, and the result — whatever it is — will tell you something true about your search.
