# How to Build a Great Prep Brief

startingmonday.app | May 2026

---

The prep brief gets better as your context gets richer. A brief generated from a LinkedIn import and a company name produces something useful. A brief generated from a current resume, the job description, a recent annual report, three years of company signals, and the notes from your last two conversations with contacts at the company produces something that makes you look like the most prepared person in the room.

The difference is not the AI model. It is the context you feed it.

---

## What Goes Into the Brief

The brief draws from everything attached to a company workspace plus everything in your profile. Understanding what each element contributes helps you prioritize what to add when time is short.

Your resume is the foundation. It provides your record, your career arc, the specific mandates you have carried, and the outcomes you have produced. The brief cannot connect your background to the company's situation without it. Upload your most current and comprehensive version. You can tailor specific versions for specific roles later — the base version should include everything.

The job description, when you have it, sharpens the brief dramatically. It tells the system exactly what the company is looking for and allows the brief to align your specific experience to their specific requirements, section by section. If you do not have a formal job description, paste the relevant context from a posting, a LinkedIn description, or even notes from a recruiter conversation into a document and attach it.

Company documents — the annual report, a strategic plan, an earnings transcript, recent press — provide context the AI cannot reliably synthesize from public data alone. An annual report gives the CFO language and the strategic priorities the board has publicly committed to. An earnings transcript shows what the CEO emphasizes under pressure. A recent industry report frames the competitive context the company is operating in. These documents take ten minutes to find and attach. They change the quality of the brief meaningfully.

Signals from the past six to twelve months are automatically incorporated. You do not need to add these manually — they surface as the system monitors the company and are reflected in the brief. This is why companies with a longer monitoring history produce better briefs: the signal history is richer.

Contact notes are the last layer. If you have had conversations with people at this company — the CFO, a board member, someone in IT leadership — and you have logged those conversations with notes, those notes appear in the brief as context for the relationship questions. The brief knows that you have a contact who described the company's ERP frustration eighteen months ago and surfaces it when you are walking into a conversation about technology transformation.

---

## Building Before the Call

Start the company workspace the moment you add the company to your pipeline, not the day before the call. Every day of monitoring adds signal history. Every document you attach is context you will not have to find again.

When you receive a call invitation or a recruiter reaches out about a company on your list, the workspace should already have something in it. Your job in the 48 hours before the call is to add what is missing — the job description if you have it, the annual report if you have not attached it, any notes from contacts — and then generate the brief.

Generate the brief the day of the call, not the night before. Signals surface in real time. A brief generated four days before your call will not include the acquisition announcement that went out yesterday morning. The brief generated an hour before your call will.

---

## Reading the Brief

The brief streams section by section. Read it in order, but do not read it passively — read it looking for the intersection points. Where does the company's stated priority connect to something specific you have done? Where does a likely question surface a gap you should address proactively?

Mark two or three things from the brief that you want to bring up in the conversation, not because you are planning to recite the brief but because you have identified the moments where your background is most directly relevant to their situation. Walk in knowing those moments exist. Let the conversation take you there naturally.

After the call, note what the brief got right and what it would have been sharper with. That feedback tells you what to add. A brief that accurately identified the company's primary pain point produced value. If it missed something you learned in the call, add that context to the workspace before the next conversation.

---

## When the Brief Surprises You

The brief is most valuable when it surfaces something you did not know. A recent leadership hire that changes the reporting structure. A signal from eight months ago that connects to a question you were asked in a pre-screening call. A technology stack detail buried in a 10-K that explains the company's integration complexity.

These surprises are the proof that the brief is doing something you could not do alone in the time available. They are also the signal that the system is working. When a brief surprises you consistently, you have built the context layer well.

When a brief says something that seems wrong — a claim about the company's priorities that does not match what you know, a question prediction that feels off — note it and add the correct context. The brief draws on what it has. If what it has is incomplete or outdated, add the accurate information as a document or a note. The next generation will reflect it.

---

## Before Every Conversation

Generate the brief before every first call, every second call, and every conversation where new information might have surfaced since you last looked. The brief does not cost you anything to regenerate. The cost of walking in with stale preparation is measured differently.
