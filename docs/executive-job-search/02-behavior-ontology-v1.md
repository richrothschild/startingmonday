# Executive Job Search Behavior Ontology v1

Updated: 2026-05-31

## Purpose

Define the atomic behaviors that shape an executive job transition so Starting Monday can diagnose, predict, and intervene.

## Behavioral Domains

### 1. Opportunity Sensing
How the candidate notices and filters opportunities.

Sub-behaviors:
- scans known channels vs broad discovery
- filters by title, company, geography, comp, or challenge
- tests market temperature through a small set of probes
- attends to timing cues from board, recruiter, peers, or current employer

Observed signals:
- number of sources monitored
- time from signal to first action
- rejection of low-fit roles without engaging
- over-reliance on one channel

### 2. Positioning and Narrative
How the candidate explains who they are and why they are relevant.

Sub-behaviors:
- crafts a change story
- selects proof points
- frames scope and scale
- adjusts narrative by audience
- distinguishes leader identity from functional identity

Observed signals:
- resume iteration count
- repeated rewriting of headline / summary
- consistency across LinkedIn, résumé, and interview answers
- evidence density in examples

### 3. Outreach and Network Activation
How the candidate activates relationships and uses social capital.

Sub-behaviors:
- identifies first-degree and second-degree contacts
- chooses who to contact first
- decides ask type: referral, advice, introduction, market intel
- follows up or drops threads
- escalates from weak ties to stronger sponsorship

Observed signals:
- outreach cadence
- response rate
- follow-up latency
- proportion of warm vs cold outreach

### 4. Interview Preparation and Performance
How the candidate prepares for and performs in interviews.

Sub-behaviors:
- builds a role-specific hypothesis
- rehearses leadership stories
- anticipates objections
- calibrates detail depth
- improves after feedback

Observed signals:
- preparation artifacts created before interviews
- repetition of mock answers
- note-taking after interviews
- speed of revision between rounds

### 5. Decision and Evaluation
How the candidate chooses whether to pursue, continue, or accept.

Sub-behaviors:
- compares company, challenge, compensation, and context
- evaluates risk against identity and timing
- asks for clarifying data
- delays or accelerates based on personal context
- decides when ambiguity is acceptable

Observed signals:
- offer comparison style
- number of decision criteria used
- willingness to proceed on a maybe
- time spent stalled after offer

### 6. Regulation and Resilience
How the candidate handles rejection, uncertainty, and pressure.

Sub-behaviors:
- recovers from rejection
- avoids perfection loops
- maintains momentum through ambiguity
- seeks coaching or social support
- tolerates loss of current identity

Observed signals:
- drop-off after rejection
- procrastination spikes
- over-editing frequency
- coaching engagement rate

### 7. Role-Transition Adaptation
How the candidate adjusts to the new role after acceptance.

Sub-behaviors:
- learns new stakeholder map
- shifts from search mode to integration mode
- establishes early wins
- manages new board or peer expectations
- renegotiates time and attention

Observed signals:
- first-90-day planning depth
- stakeholder schedule planning
- early-win definition quality
- frequency of check-ins

## Executive-Level Cross-Cutting Variables

- role seniority
- functional identity strength
- board visibility
- family / geography constraints
- urgency of transition
- voluntary vs forced transition
- passive vs active search posture
- network strength
- confidence level
- tolerance for ambiguity

## Candidate State Model

Represent a candidate at any moment as:

- role segment
- transition type
- stage
- primary behavior domain
- confidence tier
- active friction
- next best intervention
