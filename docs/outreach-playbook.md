# The Outreach Playbook

startingmonday.app | May 2026

---

Most executive outreach fails before the recipient reads the second sentence. Not because the sender is unqualified. Because the message signals the wrong things: that the sender needs something, that they have not done their homework, or that this is a form letter sent to forty people with different names in the subject line.

Good outreach at the executive level is different in kind, not just in polish. This is how to do it.

---

## The Core Principle

You are not asking for a job. You are initiating a professional conversation between two peers who have a reason to talk. That reason must be real, specific, and visible in the message. If you cannot name the reason in one sentence, you do not have a reason — you have a request dressed up as a conversation.

Every outreach message you send should be explainable as: "I reached out because [specific, concrete reason]. I am asking because [specific, honest ask]." If either blank cannot be filled with something real, do not send it.

---

## Types of Outreach

There are three fundamentally different outreach situations in an executive search, and they require different approaches.

Warm outreach is to someone you know. It is a reactivation of an existing relationship, not an introduction. The message acknowledges the relationship honestly, updates them on where you are, and asks something specific. It should not read like a template. If you have known someone for ten years and your outreach to them sounds like a cover letter, they will notice and it will cost you.

Referral outreach is to someone you do not know but have a mutual connection to. The mutual connection should be named in the first line, ideally with their permission. "Sarah Brennan suggested I reach out — she thought our paths might cross given my work in PE-backed transformation." That sentence does more work than three paragraphs of credentials. Use it and then get to the point.

Cold outreach is to someone you do not know and have no connection to. It is the hardest to do well and the most commonly done badly. The only cold outreach that works is outreach that demonstrates you have earned the conversation by knowing something specific about the person or their company that the average candidate would not know. A generic cold message to a PE operating partner is noise. A message that references a portfolio company shift, a recent acquisition, or a publicly stated strategic priority gets read.

---

## What the Message Must Do

Every outreach message, regardless of type, must do four things: establish who you are in one line, give a credible and specific reason for the outreach, make a clear and low-friction ask, and not overstay its welcome.

The "who you are" line is not your full career history. It is the one sentence that tells this specific person why your background is relevant to them. For a PE operating partner focused on technology transformation, "I've run four ERP and digital transformation programs across PE-backed companies in the $200M to $1B range" is the right sentence. "I am a CIO with 20 years of experience in technology leadership" is not.

The reason for outreach should be specific enough that the recipient cannot imagine you sending the same message to anyone else. Referencing a recent company signal — a new acquisition, a leadership change, a public statement about their strategy — demonstrates that you have been paying attention and that you have a point of view. "I saw Acme Corp announced the acquisition of Northbrook last week — given what I know about integration complexity in manufacturing technology stacks, I thought this might be a conversation worth having" is a real reason. "I have long admired your firm's portfolio approach" is not.

The ask should be small, specific, and easy to say yes to. A 20-minute call is better than an in-person meeting. "Your read on whether there's a fit" is better than "any opportunities you are aware of." The easier the ask, the higher the response rate. If the person says yes to the small ask and the conversation goes well, the bigger ask follows naturally.

Length: five to eight sentences for email. Three to five sentences for LinkedIn. A message longer than that signals that you need more from this conversation than you are giving, and recipients feel it.

---

## Timing and Signals

The best outreach is timed to something real. A signal fires at a company you have been watching — a new CFO hire that typically precedes a technology assessment, a funding round that historically precedes infrastructure investment, an acquisition that creates integration complexity. The candidate who reaches out to their contact at that company the same day the signal fires is having a different conversation than the one who reaches out six weeks later with nothing specific to say.

This is why monitoring matters. When you know what is happening at your target companies before most people do, your outreach has a reason that the recipient did not expect and cannot dismiss.

---

## The Follow-Up

The first message is not the whole conversation. Most responses to executive outreach come after the second or third contact, not the first.

If someone does not respond within ten business days, follow up once with a brief, direct message that acknowledges the first and gives them a clean reason to respond or not. "Checking in on my note from October 14 — happy to connect whenever timing is right, or to step back if the timing doesn't work." That is the full message. Do not explain yourself further. Do not apologize.

If there is still no response, let it rest for 60 days and try again with a new signal or a new reason. Some relationships take three touches over six months before they activate. Track each attempt with a date so you do not lose track of where you are with each contact.

The outreach drafting tool generates a personalized message based on the contact's role and company, recent signals at their organization, and your positioning and relevant background. It produces a draft you own and edit — not something you send verbatim.
