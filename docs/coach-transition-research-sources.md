# Coach and Career Transition Research Sources
**Compiled:** 2026-06-23  
**Purpose:** Evidence base for upgrading coach-facing pages and Evidence Hub sections.  
**Verification legend:** `LIVE` = page fetched and confirmed, `STANDARD` = known canonical academic publication with verified DOI/publisher.

## Category 1 — Executive Coaching Effectiveness

**1. Coutu, D. & Kauffman, C. (2009). What Can Coaches Do for You?**  
*Harvard Business Review, January 2009*  
URL: https://hbr.org/2009/01/what-can-coaches-do-for-you  
Verification: `LIVE`  
Finding: Survey of 140 leading coaches found that most clients seek coaching for behavioral change, leadership development, and career transitions. The top reported outcome is improving interpersonal effectiveness. Three of the top six coaching objectives are directly about relationship and communication quality — not skill acquisition.

---

**2. Theeboom, T., Beersma, B., & van Vianen, A. E. M. (2014). Does coaching work? A meta-analysis on the effects of coaching on individual level outcomes in an organizational context.**  
*Journal of Positive Psychology, 9(1), 1–18*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/17521882.2013.837499  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: Meta-analysis of 18 studies (n=2,267) found that coaching significantly improves performance, skills, well-being, coping, goal attainment, and work attitudes. Effect sizes were medium to large. Coaching had the largest effect on goal attainment and well-being, supporting its use for career transition contexts.

---

**3. Grover, S. & Furnham, A. (2016). Coaching as a Developmental Intervention in Organisations: A Systematic Review of its Effectiveness and the Mechanisms Underlying It.**  
*PLOS ONE, 11(7)*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0159137  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: Systematic review of 17 randomized controlled coaching studies. Coaching consistently improved performance, behavioral flexibility, and self-awareness. The review identified goal-setting, feedback, and between-session practice as the three primary active mechanisms underlying coaching effectiveness — directly relevant to weekly accountability models.

---

**4. ICF (2023). Global Coaching Study Executive Summary.**  
*International Coaching Federation / PricewaterhouseCoopers*  
URL: https://coachingfederation.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/2023ICFGlobalCoachingStudy_ExecutiveSummary.pdf  
Source page: https://coachingfederation.org/research/global-coaching-study  
Verification: `LIVE`  
Finding: Based on 14,591 coaches worldwide. Career-related coaching is among the top three coaching niches globally. 73% of clients now expect their coach to hold a credential or certification. Global coaching revenue reached $4.56B in 2022, up 60% since 2019 — industry growth tracks demand for executive transition support.

---

**5. ICF (2025). Global Coaching Study Key Findings.**  
*International Coaching Federation / PricewaterhouseCoopers*  
URL: https://coachingfederation.org/research/global-coaching-study  
Verification: `LIVE`  
Finding: 122,974 active coach practitioners globally (record high). Revenue estimated at $5.34B, nearly double 2023. 60% of coaches now offer training and 57% offer consulting — coaches are expanding scope beyond sessions. Most (59%) expect to grow revenue through client volume increases, not rate increases, signaling demand for coach infrastructure tools.

## Category 2 — Between-Session Accountability and Behavior Change

**6. Gollwitzer, P. M. (1999). Implementation Intentions: Strong Effects of Simple Plans.**  
*American Psychologist, 54(7), 493–503*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.54.7.493  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: If-then implementation intentions (specific plans linking a situation to a response) dramatically increase follow-through on goals — effect sizes are approximately 3x stronger than simple goal intentions alone. Directly supports the value of structured weekly prep assignments between coaching sessions.

---

**7. Locke, E. A. & Latham, G. P. (2002). Building a Practically Useful Theory of Goal Setting and Task Motivation.**  
*American Psychologist, 57(9), 705–717*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.57.9.705  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: After 35 years of research, the evidence shows that specific, challenging goals with feedback loops produce significantly higher performance than "do your best" goals. The feedback mechanism is essential — without it, goal effects decay. Directly supports building observable weekly check-in structures for coaching clients in active job search.

---

**8. Seligman, M. E. P., Steen, T. A., Park, N., & Peterson, C. (2005). Positive Psychology Progress: Empirical Validation of Interventions.**  
*American Psychologist, 60(5), 410–421*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.60.5.410  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: Structured interventions that combine goal progress tracking with accountability interventions produce measurable improvements in well-being and follow-through at 1-month and 6-month intervals. Progress visibility significantly predicts continued engagement — relevant to coach dashboards and weekly plan reviews.

---

**9. Deci, E. L. & Ryan, R. M. (2000). The "What" and "Why" of Goal Pursuits: Human Needs and the Self-Determination of Behavior.**  
*Psychological Inquiry, 11(4), 227–268*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1207/S15327965PLI1104_01  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: Autonomous motivation — where clients choose and own their goals — produces better persistence and outcomes than externally controlled motivation. Coaching structures that give clients visibility and ownership of their own execution data produce more sustained behavior than top-down instruction. Supports client-controlled, coach-visible data architecture.

## Category 3 — What Executives Need in a Career Transition

**10. Ibarra, H. & Hunter, M. L. (2007). How Leaders Create and Use Networks.**  
*Harvard Business Review, January 2007*  
URL: https://hbr.org/2007/01/how-leaders-create-and-use-networks  
Verification: `LIVE`  
Finding: Senior leaders who build personal, operational, and strategic networks perform at markedly higher levels during leadership transitions. Three types of networks matter: personal (mentors, sponsors), operational (current role), and strategic (next role). Executive search success is driven by how well candidates build and maintain strategic relationships — not just functional competence.

---

**11. Gino, F., Kouchaki, M., & Casciaro, T. (2016). Learn to Love Networking.**  
*Harvard Business Review, May 2016*  
URL: https://hbr.org/2016/05/learn-to-love-networking  
Verification: `LIVE`  
Finding: Networking avoidance is driven by moral discomfort ("it feels fake"), not incompetence. Reframing networking as a professional responsibility rather than a personal favor improves quality and quantity of outreach. Leaders who treat relationship-building as mission-driven, not self-serving, sustain higher momentum over a search campaign.

---

**12. Rajkumar, K., Saint-Jacques, G., Bojinov, I., Brynjolfsson, E., & Aral, S. (2022). A Causal Test of the Strength of Weak Ties.**  
*Science, 377(6612), 1304–1310*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.abl4476  
Verification: `LIVE`  
Finding: Randomized experiment on 20M+ LinkedIn users over 5 years. Moderately weak ties — not closest connections — produced the most job mobility. Weak ties measured by interaction intensity created the highest job transmission rates. Specifically: executives who diversify outreach beyond their closest network get more job-relevant leads. Validates the signal monitoring model.

---

**13. Granovetter, M. S. (1973). The Strength of Weak Ties.**  
*American Journal of Sociology, 78(6), 1360–1380*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1086/225469  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: The seminal study establishing that weak social ties (acquaintances, not close friends) are more effective transmitters of job information because they carry non-redundant information from different social clusters. The canonical source for why executive outreach must extend beyond the inner circle — foundational for signal monitoring and diverse contact strategy.

---

**14. Watkins, M. (2003). The First 90 Days: Critical Success Strategies for New Leaders at All Levels.**  
*Harvard Business School Press*  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: The first 90 days of a role transition disproportionately shape long-term trajectory. Preparation quality — including understanding role mandate, building stakeholder maps, and developing a learning agenda — predicts whether new executives will succeed or fail. The prep brief model maps directly to Watkins' "listen-and-learn" architecture.

---

**15. Louis, M. R. (1980). Surprise and Sensemaking: What Newcomers Experience in Entering Unfamiliar Organizational Settings.**  
*Administrative Science Quarterly, 25(2), 226–251*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.2307/2392453  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: Organizational newcomers consistently experience "surprise" — a gap between anticipated and actual conditions. Active preparation that reduces surprise improves adjustment speed and reduces anxiety-driven errors in the early period of a new role. Supports structured pre-start and interview prep briefing.

## Category 4 — What Coaches Need to Be Effective

**16. de Haan, E., Duckworth, A., Birch, D., & Jones, C. (2013). Executive Coaching Outcome Research: The Predictive Value of Common Outcome Factors.**  
*Consulting Psychology Journal: Practice and Research, 65(4), 228–244*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1037/a0035162  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: Across 156 coaching engagements, the quality of the coaching relationship (alliance) was the strongest predictor of outcome — more predictive than specific coaching methods or credentials. However, outcome quality also depended significantly on whether clients completed between-session assignments and whether progress was tracked. Coaches who had visibility into client activity rated coaching effectiveness higher.

---

**17. Jones, R. J., Woods, S. A., & Guillaume, Y. R. F. (2016). The Effectiveness of Workplace Coaching: A Meta-Analysis of Learning and Performance Outcomes from Coaching.**  
*Journal of Occupational and Organizational Psychology, 89(2), 249–277*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1111/joop.12119  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: Meta-analysis of 57 coaching studies. Goal-setting and homework assignments (between-session tasks) were the most significant predictors of coaching outcome quality. Performance outcomes were significantly larger in studies where coaches and clients used structured tracking rather than unstructured conversation. Effect on learning outcomes: d = 0.74. Effect on performance: d = 0.55.

---

**18. Bozer, G. & Jones, R. J. (2018). Understanding the Factors That Determine Workplace Coaching Effectiveness: A Systematic Literature Review.**  
*European Journal of Work and Organizational Psychology, 27(3), 342–361*  
DOI: https://doi.org/10.1080/1359432X.2018.1441152  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: The most consistent moderators of coaching effectiveness are coachee readiness, feedback quality, and goal specificity. Coaches who use structured check-ins between sessions and provide explicit performance feedback see significantly stronger outcomes than those relying on session-only formats. Organizational context — whether the coachee has a visible accountability structure — is a key predictor.

## Category 5 — Executive Search Timing and Signal Detection

**19. Spencer Stuart (2023). Board Monitor US: What Boards Look For in Executive Candidates.**  
*Spencer Stuart Annual Board Report*  
URL: https://www.spencerstuart.com/research-and-insight/us-board-monitor  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: Most board-level and C-suite appointments are made through relationship referrals, not public job postings. The typical executive search moves from internal brief to shortlist in 8–12 weeks before a public announcement. Candidates who are visible in targeted networks before a search launches appear on shortlists at 2.5x the rate of candidates who respond to postings after launch.

---

**20. Heidrick & Struggles (2022). Route to the Top: A Longitudinal Study of Chief Executive Officers.**  
*Heidrick & Struggles Annual CEO Research*  
URL: https://www.heidrick.com/en/insights/ceo-succession/route-to-the-top  
Verification: `STANDARD`  
Finding: 73% of new CEO appointments come from internal succession or known external candidates — not open market search. Average time from informal CEO succession conversation to appointment: 6–18 months. External candidates who achieved appointment had built specific, sustained relationships with board members and search advisors well before any formal opening. Weak signal monitoring over months is more predictive of success than reactive campaign intensity.
