# Starting Monday — Marketing Plan

startingmonday.app | May 2026

---

## Strategic Position

Starting Monday does not market to people looking for jobs. It markets to senior professionals running a search campaign — a meaningfully different audience with a meaningfully different psychology.

The language is campaign language, not job-seeker language. The tone is operational, not motivational. The product does not celebrate effort; it produces outcomes. Every marketing touchpoint should reinforce that Starting Monday is infrastructure for a serious search, not a tool to apply faster.

One-sentence positioning: Starting Monday is the operating system for a senior professional's job search — intelligence before roles are posted, precise relationship management, and preparation that wins the first conversation.

---

## Target Audience

The primary audience is the active VP and C-suite candidate: VP, SVP, CIO, CTO, COO, CFO, and equivalent titles earning $250,000 to $1 million or more in total compensation. They are actively searching or genuinely open to the right opportunity. Their pain is not a lack of credentials — it is the gap between what they know they are worth and how poorly the search process serves them at their level. They live on LinkedIn as passive scrollers, not active applicants, and they move through referral networks, professional associations, alumni programs, and outplacement programs.

The secondary audience is the passive monitor: the same title range, not yet actively searching, willing to pay $49 per month to stay informed and keep relationships warm. They convert to the Active plan at the moment they decide to commit. The Passive tier exists specifically for this persona.

The tertiary audience is the level-up candidate: Director to VP, VP to CIO. Ready for the promotion that requires changing companies, more uncertain about positioning, and with the highest need for the strategy brief and AI chat among any segment.

## Go-to-Market: Phase 1

The objective is twenty-five paying customers who represent the core persona. The more important goal is understanding what creates the moment of "this is exactly what I needed." Stories from that moment are the foundation of everything that follows.

The primary channel is the founder's personal network. The founder is the ideal user. Every conversation with a peer who says "I'm in search" is a direct conversion opportunity. This is not a referral program; it is the founder selling personally to people they know and respect.

The secondary channels are LinkedIn personal brand and university alumni networks. The founder posts about the operational realities of senior search — not tips, not job advice, but observations that resonate with executives who have been through it. The product is mentioned in context, not promoted. Alumni career offices at Wake Forest, Stanford, Wharton, and Harvard Business School work directly with senior candidates in transition; introductions through those offices put Starting Monday in front of the right people at the right moment.

Outreach to executives who have recently been through outplacement programs — Lee Hecht Harrison, Right Management, Challenger Gray — is also a Phase 1 channel. Former outplacement clients know the gap. A direct, honest message about what Starting Monday does differently converts well.

## Go-to-Market: Phase 2

The objective is one hundred paying customers and a repeatable acquisition channel. Phase 2 is about establishing Starting Monday as a credible voice on executive search, not as a product that advertises at its audience.

The content strategy is built around first-person operational observations. LinkedIn articles from the founder or early users — "What I learned tracking forty companies for six months," "The prep brief that changed how I walked into interviews," "Why the role you want is not on LinkedIn" — are the primary content format. Anonymous case studies of users who converted a cold signal into a first call, or who walked into a prep brief and got the offer, provide social proof without requiring users to identify themselves. Teardowns showing what Starting Monday detected at a specific company three weeks before a role was posted demonstrate the intelligence advantage in concrete terms.

SEO content targets high-intent, low-competition queries: executive job search tools, CIO job search, VP interview preparation, how to track job opportunities. These are searches made by people who already know they need a system.

Every successful user who accepts an offer is a referral opportunity. The ask is simple: "Who else in your network is in search or thinking about it?" One introduction from a satisfied CIO is worth more than ten posts. A formal referral program — one free month for the referrer and one month added to the referee's trial — systematizes what should happen organically.

## Go-to-Market: Phase 3

The objective is five hundred customers and category leadership for serious executive search. Phase 3 is built around three partnership channels.

The Manager Tools conversation is the highest-potential single initiative. Mark Horstman and Michael Auzenne have built the largest audience of managers and executives focused on professional effectiveness. That audience is Starting Monday's audience. The conversation with Mark should not be a sponsorship pitch; it should be a peer conversation about whether the product is advice-defensible. Manager Tools does not endorse anything it has not vetted, and that rigor is what makes its recommendations credible. If the product fits, the options are a podcast episode, newsletter mention, or affiliate arrangement. What needs to be true first: the product is stable, the user experience is excellent, and enough user success stories exist to anchor the conversation.

Outplacement firm partnerships give access to large cohorts of motivated users at the moment of peak urgency. Enterprise outplacement firms are increasingly looking for technology to modernize their offerings. A white-label or partner arrangement is the structure; revenue share or per-user fee is the model. The institutional channel requires almost no CAC per seat.

Executive association sponsorships round out Phase 3. EVANTA (the CIO and CISO network), The CIO Network, SIM (Society for Information Management), and the CIO.com newsletter all reach the target audience at $2,000 to $5,000 per month, significantly below the cost per lead of broad digital advertising.

## Pricing Strategy

Current pricing is below market. For a professional earning $400,000 or above, $199 per month is less than one hour of their time. The framing should never be "is this affordable?" It should be "is this worth it?"

The anchor is the cost of a mis-positioned search. A search that extends three to six months beyond its natural duration costs $100,000 to $200,000 in foregone earnings at $400,000 annual compensation. Starting Monday at $199 per month for six months costs $1,200. That framing does not require the user to be persuaded; it requires them to do arithmetic.

Do not discount. Offering a lower price to someone who has not yet experienced the product signals that the product is not confident in its value. The free trial is the conversion mechanism, not price reduction. Quarterly billing reduces churn and rewards commitment; promote it as the default for users who are engaged.

The Executive and Campaign waitlists serve two functions: they signal demand at the premium tiers, and they create a pipeline of self-identified high-value customers ready to convert when those tiers launch.

## Messaging Framework

What Starting Monday says:

"Your next role isn't on a job board."
"At this level, the search is a campaign, not an application."
"Know before anyone else."
"Walk in prepared."
"The best CIO mandates are created, not posted."
"30-day pilot. No credit card."

What Starting Monday does not say:

"Land your dream job" — generic, does not fit the audience.
"Apply to hundreds of jobs faster" — volume is the wrong frame.
"AI-powered resume builder" — undersells the product, wrong category.
"Career coaching" — implies dependence on someone else's judgment.
"Job board" — never.

The tone is spare, direct, and confident. The copy should read as if it was written by someone who has run a search at this level and knows exactly what the audience is dealing with. No exclamation points. No "excited to announce." No emoji in marketing materials.

## Metrics

| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Paying customers | 25 | 100 | 500 |
| Monthly trial starts | 10 | 50 | 200 |
| Trial to paid conversion | 30% | 35% | 40% |

| NPS (paying users) | 50 | 55 | 60 |
| Active-to-Passive ratio | 2:1 | 2:1 | 2:1 |

The most important metric in Phase 1 is not revenue. It is stories. How many users can describe a specific moment where Starting Monday gave them an advantage? Those stories are the product's proof of concept and the foundation of everything that comes after.

## The Founder's Unfair Advantage

The founder is an active CIO in search — the exact target user. This is not a theoretical product built for an assumed persona. It is a tool built by and for someone running the exact process the product is designed to support.

That authenticity is the product's most powerful marketing asset. Every conversation, post, and case study should draw on it. The marketing is not "we built a tool for executives in search." The marketing is "I was in search, this is what I built to run it, and it works."

That story is rare. Use it.
