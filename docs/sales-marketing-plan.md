# Starting Monday — Sales and Marketing Plan

startingmonday.app | May 2026

---

## Strategic Position

Starting Monday does not market to people looking for jobs. It markets to senior professionals running a search campaign. That distinction is not semantic — it is the product's entire positioning. The language is campaign language, not job-seeker language. The tone is operational, not motivational. The product does not celebrate effort; it produces outcomes.

One-sentence positioning: Starting Monday is the operating system for a senior professional's search — intelligence before roles are posted, precise relationship management, and preparation that wins the first conversation.

---

## Target Audience

The primary audience is the active VP and C-suite candidate: VP, SVP, CIO, CTO, COO, CFO, and equivalent titles earning $250,000 to $1 million or more in total compensation. They are actively searching or genuinely open to the right opportunity. Their pain is not a lack of credentials — it is the gap between what they know they are worth and how poorly the search process serves them at their level.

The secondary audience is the passive monitor: the same title range, not actively searching, willing to pay $49 per month to stay informed and keep relationships warm. They convert to Active at the moment they commit to a search.

The tertiary audience is the level-up candidate: Director to VP, VP to CIO. Ready for the promotion that requires changing companies. The highest need for the strategy brief and AI chat among any current segment.

## Individual Acquisition (B2C)

### Phase 1: First 25 Paying Customers

The objective is twenty-five paying customers who represent the core persona. The more important goal is understanding what creates the moment of "this is exactly what I needed." Stories from that moment are the foundation of everything that follows.

The primary channel is the founder's personal network. The founder is the ideal user. Every conversation with a peer who says "I'm in search" is a direct conversion opportunity. This is not a referral program — it is the founder selling personally to people they know and respect.

Secondary channels are LinkedIn personal brand and university alumni networks, specifically Wake Forest, Stanford, Wharton, and Harvard Business School. Alumni career offices work directly with senior candidates in transition. The founder posts about the operational realities of senior search — not tips, not job advice, but observations that resonate with executives who have been through it. The product is mentioned in context, not promoted.

Outreach to executives who have recently been through outplacement programs is also a Phase 1 channel. Former outplacement clients know the gap. A direct, honest message about what Starting Monday does differently converts well.

### Phase 2: First 100 Paying Customers

The objective is one hundred paying customers and a repeatable acquisition channel. Phase 2 is about establishing Starting Monday as a credible voice on executive search, not as a product that advertises at its audience.

The content strategy is built around first-person operational observations. LinkedIn articles from the founder or early users — "What I learned tracking forty companies for six months," "The prep brief that changed how I walked into interviews," "Why the role you want is not on LinkedIn" — are the primary content format. Anonymous case studies of users who converted a cold signal into a first call provide social proof without requiring users to identify themselves. Teardowns showing what Starting Monday detected at a specific company before a role was posted demonstrate the intelligence advantage concretely.

SEO content targets high-intent, low-competition queries: executive job search tools, CIO job search, VP interview preparation, how to track job opportunities. These are searches made by people who already know they need a system.

Every successful user who accepts an offer is a referral opportunity. The post-completion email asks: "Who else in your network is in search?" One introduction from a satisfied CIO is worth more than ten posts. A formal referral program — one free month for the referrer and one month added to the referee's trial — systematizes what should happen organically.

### Phase 3: 500 Paying Customers and Category Leadership

The objective is five hundred customers and recognition as the serious alternative to the traditional outplacement model. Phase 3 is built around three partnership channels.

The Manager Tools conversation is the highest-potential single initiative. Mark Horstman and Michael Auzenne have built the largest audience of managers and executives focused on professional effectiveness. That audience is Starting Monday's audience. The conversation is not a sponsorship pitch — it is a peer conversation about whether the product is advice-defensible. What needs to be true first: the product is stable, the user experience is excellent, and enough user success stories exist to anchor the conversation. If the product fits, the options are a podcast episode, newsletter mention, or affiliate arrangement.

Executive association sponsorships reach the target audience directly: EVANTA (the CIO and CISO network), The CIO Network, SIM (Society for Information Management), and the CIO.com newsletter all reach the target audience at $2,000 to $5,000 per month, significantly below the cost per lead of broad digital advertising.

## Institutional Acquisition (B2B)

The institutional channels — outplacement firms, university alumni offices, and corporate HR transition benefits — produce large cohorts of the right users at the right moment with near-zero marketing cost per lead. They are also slower to close and require a different sales capability than the individual acquisition channels. The right approach is to start lightweight, validate with real users, and formalize only after the pattern is proven.

### The B2B Sales Motion

There are five structural options for building the institutional channel. None is sufficient alone. The right approach sequences them.

Founder-led sales is the highest-leverage starting point. The founder's story — "I built this for myself while in search" — is uniquely credible with outplacement firm leaders and university career directors. No other person on the team can open a door with the same authenticity. The cost is founder time. The right scope for founder-led B2B is the first three to five institutional relationships, not as a permanent model.

A dedicated enterprise sales hire brings expertise and frees the founder for product. The right timing is after the first two or three institutional relationships are in place and the pattern of the sale is understood. Before that, a sales hire has no playbook to work from and no pipeline to accelerate. Expect $150,000 to $200,000 fully loaded, 12-month payback period, and 18 months to full productivity. Do not hire before the pilot results are in hand.

An industry advisor with outplacement or HR relationships can compress the entry cycle significantly. A fractional advisor with 20 years in the outplacement industry can make introductions that would otherwise take 18 months of cold outreach. The structure matters: equity plus a cash component tied to closed deals. This is likely the best first move after the founder-led pilots — it accelerates without the overhead of a full hire.

An HR technology reseller or channel partner can distribute Starting Monday through existing institutional relationships on a revenue-share model. The cost is 20 to 30 percent of revenue and partial control of positioning. For a product whose brand is closely tied to the founder's credibility, a generic reseller risks the positioning. Worth exploring once the product is proven, not before.

Running a pilot with one firm before building anything is the prerequisite for all of the above. A single pilot with one mid-sized outplacement boutique — ten free seats, 60-day evaluation, a debrief conversation — produces more useful information than any amount of planning. It reveals what the institutional buyer actually cares about, what the product is missing for that use case, and whether institutionally-sourced users engage as well as self-directed users. The pilot is a learning event, not a revenue event.

Sequencing: one boutique pilot now, one industry advisor identified in the next 60 days, founder leads the first three real institutional relationships, hire a dedicated B2B person when institutional revenue justifies the role.

### Implementation Plan: 10 Outplacement and University Approaches

The ten approaches below are ordered from fastest cycle to slowest. None requires a formal contract. All can run in parallel.

**Immediate (Weeks 1–2)**

Approach 1: Reach the practitioners before the firms. Individual outplacement career coaches — not the firms they work for — make the recommendation in the session with the executive. Identify 50 coaches on LinkedIn using title searches ("executive career coach," "outplacement consultant," "VP-level career coach"). Send a direct, personal note offering 90 days of free Active access for the coach's personal use. A coach who has generated a prep brief for their own background will recommend it. No contract, no procurement, no firm approval needed.

Action: Build a list of 50 coaches. Draft the outreach message. Begin sending 10 per week.

Success metric: 15 coaches accept free access within 30 days. At least 3 refer a client within 60 days.

Approach 2: Build a coach referral link. Create a landing page and unique referral link for any coach willing to share it. Clients referred by a coach get a 60-day trial instead of 30. The coach gets a referral dashboard showing how many clients used it. No fee, no agreement, no integration required.

Action: Build the landing page and referral tracking (UTM parameters + Supabase referral_source field). Create the 60-day trial override for referred signups.

Success metric: 10 coaches have active referral links within 45 days.

Approach 3: Create the resource kit. A two-page PDF and landing page titled "Running a disciplined executive search: what works at the VP and above level." Starting Monday is mentioned as the tool that operationalizes the approach. Coaches distribute it to clients and take credit for the recommendation. The document should stand alone as genuinely useful regardless of whether the reader signs up.

Action: Write the PDF (2 pages, Campaign Standard register). Design a landing page version. Send both to the coach list as a reason to follow up.

Success metric: 200 PDF downloads or landing page views within 60 days.

**Month 1**

Approach 4: Identify and pitch one boutique outplacement firm for a pilot. Target a firm with 5 to 20 consultants in a major market. One decision-maker, 2-week decision cycle. Offer 10 free seats for their client group, a 60-day evaluation period, and a debrief at the end. The pitch is the founder's story and one user success case. The ask is one introduction to their next cohort of executive candidates.

Action: Identify 5 boutique firms via LinkedIn and Google. Send personal notes to founders. First meeting within 30 days.

Success metric: One pilot agreement in place within 45 days.

Approach 5: Webinar as entry event. Offer to run a free 45-minute session for any firm's client group on "How to run a disciplined executive search at the VP and above level." The product is shown, not sold. The conversion happens at the end with a trial offer. The firm gets to look like they are providing a modern resource without any cost or procurement risk.

Action: Build a 45-minute presentation. Approach the boutique pilot firm with the webinar offer alongside or instead of the seat pilot. Track attendee conversion to trial.

Success metric: First webinar delivered within 45 days. 20% attendee trial conversion.

Approach 6: For universities — reach the career director directly. Do not approach the university. Approach the individual career director at Wake Forest, Stanford, Wharton, and Harvard Business School with a personal note and an offer of 10 free accounts for the office's use in advising alumni. The career director who uses Starting Monday with one executive alumnus and sees them land will tell every colleague they know.

Action: Identify 4 career directors by name. Send personal notes from the founder. Reference the founder's own alumni affiliation where applicable.

Success metric: 2 career directors accept free accounts within 30 days.

**Month 2**

Approach 7: Target HR directly for the separation-as-benefit play. Companies doing executive restructurings often want to provide transition support but cannot move fast on a formal outplacement contract. A direct note to CHROs at companies with recent public executive restructuring announcements — offering 60-day free access for departing executives — bypasses procurement entirely for groups under a certain cost threshold. The offer is framed as "a transition tool for your departing leaders, at no cost for the first cohort."

Action: Build a list of recent restructuring announcements from public companies (LinkedIn, press). Draft the CHRO outreach. Send to 20 CHROs over 30 days.

Success metric: 3 CHROs respond. 1 provides access to a departing executive cohort.

Approach 8: Co-branded landing pages without a contract. Any outplacement firm or career office willing to share it gets a page that says "[Firm Name] — Recommended Tool for Executive Candidates." Starting Monday gets the referral attribution. The firm gets to look like they are offering modern technology. No fee, no agreement, no integration.

Action: Build a template co-branded page that takes a firm name as a parameter. Include in the outreach to all firms as a no-risk option.

Success metric: 3 co-branded pages live within 60 days.

Approach 9: Alumni chapter events for university channel. Every major business school has alumni chapters in major cities. Chapter presidents can approve a sponsorship or presentation without university involvement. Offer to speak at one alumni chapter event on executive search — providing genuine value while demonstrating the product.

Action: Identify chapter presidents at 3 schools in markets where the founder has a connection. Send a personal note offering a session on executive search for senior alumni.

Success metric: 1 chapter event delivered within 90 days. 15% attendee trial conversion.

**Month 3 and Ongoing**

Approach 10: The win story as a sales asset. The most powerful sentence in any institutional conversation is: "One of your former clients used Starting Monday during their transition and landed a CIO role in 5 months. Would you like to see what they used?" Find this story. It exists. Every institutional outreach after this story is in hand is a different conversation.

Action: Identify users whose origin was referral from an outplacement context (via referral_source field). Interview the first three. Draft an anonymous case study. This becomes the institutional sales asset.

Success metric: One documented win story by day 60. Used in every institutional conversation thereafter.

## Messaging Framework

What Starting Monday says:

"Your next role isn't on a job board."
"At this level, the search is a campaign, not an application."
"Know before anyone else."
"Walk in prepared."
"The best CIO mandates are created, not posted."
"30-day pilot. No credit card."

What Starting Monday does not say:

"Land your dream job." Generic. Does not fit the audience.
"Apply to hundreds of jobs faster." Volume is the wrong frame.
"AI-powered resume builder." Undersells the product, wrong category.
"Career coaching." Implies dependence on someone else's judgment.
"Job board." Never.

The tone is spare, direct, and confident. The copy should read as if it was written by someone who has run a search at this level and knows exactly what the audience is dealing with. No exclamation points. No "excited to announce."

## The Founder's Unfair Advantage

The founder is an active CIO in search — the exact target user. This is not a theoretical product built for an assumed persona. It is a tool built by and for someone running the exact process the product is designed to support.

That authenticity is the product's most powerful marketing asset. Every conversation, post, and case study should draw on it. The marketing is not "we built a tool for executives in search." The marketing is "I was in search, this is what I built to run it, and it works."

That story is rare. Use it everywhere.
