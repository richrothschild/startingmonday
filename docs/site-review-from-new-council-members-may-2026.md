## 1. DAVID OGILVY — Advertising & Brand Strategy

**Current Grade: B+**

**What Works:**
- Hero message "Be ready. Be early." is directional and breaks through generic SaaS noise
- Dark background + orange accent creates visual distinctiveness (Ogilvy would appreciate the constraint)
- The three-step onboarding flow is clear and believable
- Free trial offer (30 days, no credit card) removes friction
- Copy assumes the reader is intelligent; you're not over-explaining
- Navigation hierarchy makes sense (Partners/Coaches visible early)

**What Doesn't Work:**
- The eyebrow "A disciplined search system for senior technology executives" is too generic. Any recruiting tool could say this.
- The body copy mixes metaphors: "spot opportunities before roles are broadly posted" then "run a disciplined campaign." These are different narratives fighting each other.
- The support lines under the hero are solid but could be 40% more concrete: "Built for the C-suite" is weaker than "Built for CIOs moving up to CEO in their first 90 days."
- No social proof or credibility marker in the hero section. Which executives are already using this? What has changed for them?
- The brand personality is unclear. Are we premium and exclusive? Or accessible and practical? The messaging waffles.
- The "Try in free" button is orange, but it's competing with too many other visual elements. The call-to-action is muddled.

**What I Dislike Most:**
- The site reads like it's selling a tool to solve a problem (searching for jobs). It should be selling a *status* and a *narrative*. Right now you're speaking to executives' pain. You should be speaking to their identity. "Be ready" gets there. But the rest of the site walks it back to "here's a job search platform."
- Missing the proof. Testimonials matter less than demonstrating the idea works. Show me a real executive's pipeline. Show me a real prep brief. Show me what "ready" looks like in practice.

**To Get an A, Change:**

1. **Strengthen the positioning statement (eyebrow).**
   - Current: "A disciplined search system for senior technology executives."
   - Better: "Signal intelligence for C-suite executives. Spot opportunities before your competitors. Prepare deeper than the field."
   - Rationale: Moves from feature (system) to benefit (signal + speed + depth). Puts the executive's competitive advantage first.

2. **Unify the narrative. Pick one story and tell it completely.**
   - Current story: "You're searching for a job and we help you search better."
   - Better story: "The strongest executives don't wait for the market to tell them it's time to move. They prepare for the conversation before it's officially open."
   - Apply this story consistently across all copy. Everything should reinforce this one idea.

3. **Add a credibility moment in the hero.**
   - Show a real testimonial from a recognizable executive (name, title, company). One sentence: what changed?
   - Or: Show a real pipeline screenshot with company names and signals. Prove this works.
   - Or: Show the number of executives who moved to new roles using Starting Monday and their average speed-to-offer.

4. **Clarify the brand personality and lock it down.**
   - You're speaking to intelligent, ambitious, confident executives. The brand voice should match: direct, no fluff, peer-to-peer, premium (not desperate).
   - Remove any language that smells like you're trying to convince them. You're not. You're stating facts about how strong executives move.
   - Example language shift: "You can track 30 companies" → "You're tracking 30 companies. Actually watching them, not wondering."

5. **Redesign the CTA hierarchy.**
   - Primary CTA: "Start Free" (orange button, prominent)
   - Secondary CTA: "See a demo" (text link, to show you have nothing to hide)
   - Don't have five CTAs competing. One primary, one fallback.

6. **Add a "why this works" section with real proof.**
   - Not features. Proof.
   - "Executives using this reach out to target companies 4.2 weeks earlier than the field."
   - "60% of users report their first interview within 30 days (vs. 39 days average for cold applications)."
   - Real numbers that prove the positioning claim.

## 2. RORY SUTHERLAND — Behavioral Economics & Lateral Thinking

**Current Grade: B**

**What Works:**
- "Be ready. Be early." creates a psychological shift: from reactive to proactive. This is good lateral thinking.
- The free trial with no credit card removes the loss-aversion barrier. Smart.
- The before/after situation cards show you understand that people think in stories, not features.
- The partner section signals exclusivity: "These other smart people trust us." This is social proof done right.
- The pricing page anchor to coaching cost ($300-500/hour) is excellent psychological positioning.

**What Doesn't Work:**
- The site doesn't create any fear of missing out (FOMO) for the right audience. You're selling on logic ("here's why it works") when you should be selling on psychology ("here's what you're missing").
- No urgency or scarcity signal. Why should an executive act *now* instead of in 6 months when they actually need it?
- The "situations" section tries to be inclusive (6 different scenarios). This is a mistake. You're appealing to everyone, which means you're appealing to no one. Pick one: "You decided to move. Now you're searching reactively." That's the gut punch.
- Missing the status play. Using this tool should make the executive feel smarter and more in control, not needier. Right now it feels like admitting "I need help searching."
- The competitive frame is weak. You're not saying "your competitors are already doing this" or "the candidates beating you for roles are already moving like this." That's the real FOMO.

**What I Dislike Most:**
- You're being too honest about what the problem is. "Searching is hard, here's a tool that makes it easier." That's not compelling. It's defensive. The real insight is: "The executives who move fastest are the ones who move *before* it's obvious they should. Everyone else is reacting." That's the insight. Lead with that.
- The site is rational when it should be emotional. Your target customer doesn't want a better job board. They want to feel like they have an unfair advantage. Right now, you're selling them a job board with better UX.

**To Get an A, Change:**

1. **Create a single, specific situation that hits the psychological nerve.**
   - Current: Six scenarios (urgent, building, VP-up, monitor, selective, returning). Scattered.
   - Better: One scenario: "My role was eliminated. I have 90 days to land well. I'm competing against people who've been searching for months."
   - Rationale: Specificity creates identification. Broad appeals create nothing.

2. **Add a competitive FOMO element.**
   - "The CIOs moving fastest right now are tracking companies 3-4 weeks before the role is public. You're still waiting for the posting."
   - "Your competitor is already preparing for the conversation before the recruiter calls. You're still wondering when to start."
   - This creates the psychological shift: from "this is helpful" to "this is necessary."

3. **Reframe the status play.**
   - Current frame: "You need help searching." (Negative status)
   - Better frame: "You have an unfair advantage because you know where the moves are happening." (Positive status)
   - Move the language from "job search tool" to "executive intelligence system." Intelligence = status.

4. **Add urgency without being pushy.**
   - Not "Sign up now!" (desperate)
   - Better: "Executives who are active in their search usually move within 90 days of starting here. The longer you wait to get started, the shorter your window."
   - Or: "The executives winning right now started preparing 6 months ago. You can start today."

5. **Add a proof moment that creates FOMO, not reassurance.**
   - Not: "3,000 executives have used this tool." (Who cares?)
   - Better: "The executives who placed fastest used Starting Monday to reach out 4 weeks before the role went to a recruiter. By then, the decision was already made."
   - Rationale: This creates the fear that you're late if you don't start now.

6. **Simplify the copy to make the edge sharper.**
   - Current copy often hedges: "helps you," "can give you," "usually ready."
   - Sharper: "The prep brief is ready in a minute. The signal is ahead of the field. The conversation is yours to lose."
   - Rationale: Specificity and directness create psychological weight.

7. **Add a "not for everyone" statement to create exclusivity.**
   - "Built for CIOs, CTOs, and senior technology leaders who are serious about their move. Not for mid-level job seekers. Not for people who just want to explore."
   - Rationale: Exclusivity creates status. Everyone wants to be on the insider team.

## 3. OCTAVIA ZAHRT — Executive Coaching & Transitions

**Current Grade: B**

**What Works:**
- The "what changes" section on the /for-coaches page shows you understand the difference between before/after coaching situations. That's good.
- The positioning of prep briefs and pipeline visibility is solid. Coaches will understand immediately how this reduces session friction.
- The free trial is smart for coaches to recommend to clients without friction.
- The 20% commission model is clear and not predatory. Coaches will see this as reasonable.
- The daily briefing concept gets at something real: executives need structure between sessions.

**What Doesn't Work:**
- The site doesn't address the emotional dimensions of transition. It's all logic and features. Executives entering transition are scared, confused, or angry. The site doesn't acknowledge that.
- Missing the "accountability" frame. Coaches will ask: "How does this keep my client accountable?" The site doesn't answer this clearly.
- The pipeline tracking feature is explained in product terms ("command center") not coaching terms ("this is where you know what your client is actually doing").
- The partner dashboard for coaches is mentioned but not shown. Coaches want to see exactly how they track commissions and client engagement.
- The site speaks to individual executives, not to coaches. There's a /for-coaches page, but it's not integrated into the main experience. A coach landing on the homepage wouldn't know this is built for them.

**What I Dislike Most:**
- The site assumes search is a rational, individual project. In reality, search is an emotional journey that requires human support. The way this tool is positioned, it sounds like it *replaces* the coach. It should be positioned as the *leverage* the coach needs.
- Missing the relationship dimension. Coaching is about relationship and accountability. The site is about features and intelligence. These are not the same thing.

**To Get an A, Change:**

1. **Create separate hero experiences for executives vs. coaches.**
   - Current: One homepage for everyone. This confuses both audiences.
   - Better: When a coach lands on the site, the hero should say: "Give your clients the infrastructure so you can focus on the strategy and relationship work."
   - Rationale: Different buyer, different value. Don't make coaches extract the value meant for them.

2. **Add an emotional acknowledgment to the main messaging.**
   - Current: All logic and features.
   - Add a line like: "Search is not just logistics. It's about confidence, clarity, and forward momentum. Starting Monday is the infrastructure that creates those."
   - Rationale: Coaches know the emotional dimension is real. Acknowledge it.

3. **Reframe features in coaching language, not product language.**
   - Current product language: "Pipeline Command Center," "AI Prep Brief," "Daily Morning Briefing"
   - Coaching language: "See what your client is actually doing," "Walk into every session knowing exactly where they stand," "The daily structure that keeps them accountable"
   - Rationale: Coaches think in terms of accountability and insight, not features.

4. **Show the partner dashboard.**
   - Create a screenshot or tour showing: commission tracking, referral link, subscriber list, activation rates.
   - Coaches want to know exactly what they'll see and how easy it is to track their clients and earnings.
   - Rationale: Transparency builds trust. Show, don't tell.

5. **Add a "what this doesn't do" section on the /for-coaches page.**
   - Explicitly state: "This doesn't replace you. It removes the research burden so you can do the work only you can do."
   - Rationale: Coaches need to know this is leverage, not replacement.

6. **Create a clear pathway for coaches to recommend this to clients.**
   - Right now, a coach has to figure out how to pitch this. Create copy they can literally copy-paste and send to their clients.
   - Example: "I'm recommending a tool that handles the daily research and tracking. It keeps you accountable between our sessions and gives us both visibility into your progress. Try it free for 30 days."
   - Rationale: Remove the friction for coaches to become advocates.

7. **Add proof from other coaches.**
   - Not generic testimonials. Specific: "I can see my clients' pipeline. Prep briefs are ready before our session. I spend 30% less time on research, 100% more time on strategy." — Coach name/credentials
   - Rationale: Coaches trust other coaches. Peer proof is the strongest proof.

## 4. CLAUDIO FERNÁNDEZ-ARÁOZ — Executive Search & C-Suite Positioning

**Current Grade: B-**

**What Works:**
- The intelligence scanner concept is sound. Early signal detection is genuinely valuable for senior searches.
- The prep brief idea is right. Senior candidates need depth of preparation, not speed of job applications.
- The positioning around "before the role is posted" is directionally correct.
- The three pricing tiers show you've thought about different user scenarios (monitoring, active, executive).

**What Doesn't Work:**
- The site doesn't convey what "ready" actually means for C-suite candidates. What is the standard? How do you know you're ready?
- The prep brief description is weak. "Win thesis, objections, peer-level questions" is generic. What does this look like for a CIO role at a PE-backed company? Be specific.
- Missing the conviction-building frame. For senior executives, the real issue is: "Do I have conviction about this company *before* I interview?" The site should lead with this.
- The pipeline feature is positioned as "tracking" when it should be positioned as "managing the relationships that matter." Different framing entirely.
- No distinction between "I'm actively searching" and "I'm preparing to search." The site treats these the same. They're not.

**What I Dislike Most:**
- You're selling activity (search faster, prepare better) when senior executives are actually buying certainty (I know this is the right move before I commit). That's the fundamental difference between mid-level job search and C-suite positioning. You're missing it.
- The site is all "what you can do" and zero "here's what the best candidates are doing." It should be aspirational. Show the model behavior, then show how your tool enables it.

**To Get an A, Change:**

1. **Define what "ready" means for C-suite candidates.**
   - Create a clear readiness framework: "You're ready when (1) you've identified which companies are actually moving (signals), (2) you understand the win thesis for each company, (3) you can articulate your value at a peer level, (4) you have a relationship pathway to the decision-maker."
   - Show how your tool helps with each of these.
   - Rationale: Senior executives need a clear model of what "ready" is. Give them one.

2. **Show real prep briefs for real roles.**
   - Pick a sample role: "CIO at a Series C SaaS company with 80% YoY growth."
   - Show the exact prep brief someone would see: win thesis, objections, peer questions, narrative frame.
   - Rationale: Specificity creates credibility. Generic descriptions sound like every other tool.

3. **Reframe the signal scanner as "conviction building," not "monitoring."**
   - Current frame: "Watch for signals." (Passive, external)
   - Better frame: "Build conviction about which companies are actually in transition *before* you talk to them." (Active, internal)
   - Rationale: This is the real value. Use it.

4. **Add a "model behavior" section.**
   - Show what the best C-suite candidates do: "They track 30 target companies. They spot signals 4-6 weeks before posting. They prepare a board-ready thesis for each one. They approach from a position of knowledge, not need."
   - Then show: "Here's how your tool enables each of those behaviors."
   - Rationale: Aspirational framing is more powerful than problem-solving framing.

5. **Create separate positioning for "still in role" vs. "actively searching."**
   - For "still in role": "Prepare while you're deciding." Monitor mode, conviction building, narrative work.
   - For "actively searching": "Move from position of strength." Active signals, prep depth, relationship velocity.
   - Rationale: These are different narratives. Treat them differently.

6. **Show the relationship between intelligence and negotiation power.**
   - "When you know the company's situation better than they expect, your negotiation position improves. You're not a candidate with a resume. You're a peer with insight."
   - Rationale: This is a senior-level benefit that mid-level job seekers don't understand. Show that you get it.

7. **Validate the "elite" positioning more aggressively.**
   - Current: "Built for CIOs, CTOs..." (factual)
   - Better: "Built for the executives who are winning competitive C-suite searches. The prep, the signals, the relationship discipline separate them from the field." (Aspirational)
   - Rationale: The best candidates want to know they're on the elite path. Signal it.

## 5. CINDY SOLOMON — Long-term Coaching & Board Development

**Current Grade: B-**

**What Works:**
- The daily briefing creates structure, which is essential for long-term engagement.
- The pipeline tracking has potential for multi-year engagement (board seeking, multiple opportunities).
- The idea of shared view access between executive and coach is solid.
- The three-tier model allows for different engagement depths.

**What Doesn't Work:**
- The site assumes all searches are 90-day sprints. Long-term positioning (6-24 months) is invisible.
- No mention of board development, governance positioning, or multi-year coaching narratives. These are separate use cases that need separate positioning.
- The pipeline feature is presented as "track relationships" when for long-term play it should be "maintain relationship momentum over years."
- Missing the multi-opportunity frame. An executive might be simultaneously pursuing a CEO role at Company A and a board seat, and advising Company B. The site doesn't address this complexity.
- The "search" framing is too short-term. For board and governance, this isn't search. It's relationship and reputation building. Different narrative entirely.

**What I Dislike Most:**
- The site treats executive transitions as tactical and short-term. The highest-value work is strategic and long-term. You're positioned in the wrong frame for your highest-value customer.
- Missing the "what maintains momentum over 18 months?" question. That's the coaching question for board seeking and senior transitions. You don't address it.

**To Get an A, Change:**

1. **Acknowledge long-term positioning as a distinct use case.**
   - Add a section or page: "Are you pursuing board seats, advisory roles, or multi-year positioning?"
   - Rationale: These buyers think differently than 90-day active searchers. Speak to them.

2. **Create a board-specific narrative.**
   - "Board seeking is a 3-5 year play. Track governance composition changes at your target companies. Build relationships with PE operating partners and current directors. Maintain your narrative as markets shift."
   - Show how the tool enables multi-year momentum without requiring constant attention.
   - Rationale: This is a huge market that's completely invisible on your current site.

3. **Reframe "pipeline" for long-term engagement.**
   - Current: "Track where each company stands." (Short-term)
   - Better: "Maintain momentum across 50+ relationships over 36 months. When you're on the board track, nothing goes cold." (Long-term)
   - Rationale: Different positioning for different timelines.

4. **Add a "what changes" section for board and advisory seeking.**
   - Before: "You're waiting for conversations to happen. You hope board opportunities surface."
   - After: "You're monitoring board composition and PE activity. You're building relationships proactively. You reach out before the conversation is expected."
   - Rationale: Show the long-term value same way you show short-term value.

5. **Add a multi-opportunity frame.**
   - "You might be pursuing CEO at Company A, considering a board seat at Company B, and advising Company C. Track all three simultaneously without losing momentum."
   - Rationale: Senior executives are often juggling multiple opportunities. Show you get it.

6. **Show the quarterly/annual rhythm for long-term plays.**
   - "Week-by-week structure keeps you engaged. Quarterly reviews show progress. Annual narratives reflect what you've learned."
   - Rationale: Long-term engagement needs a different rhythm than short-term search.

7. **Add a board-specific testimonial or case study.**
   - "I pursued a board seat for three years. Starting Monday kept me from going cold. I had a seat within four years." — Senior executive name/company
   - Rationale: Proof that this works for long-term plays is the most powerful message for this audience.

## Summary: What All Five Agree On

**Unanimous Feedback:**

1. **The core positioning ("Be ready. Be early.") is strong, but the rest of the site walks it back.** Your hero message is 90% there. The supporting copy undermines it.

2. **The site is too feature-focused and not enough narrative-focused.** You're explaining what the tool does, not what the executive becomes when they use it.

3. **You have multiple audiences (executives, coaches, search firms) but one undifferentiated message.** Create separate paths for different buyers.

4. **The credibility layer is missing.** Add proof (real stories, real numbers, real screenshots) that this works.

5. **The brand personality is unclear.** Are you premium or accessible? Urgent or patient? Pick one and lock it down.
