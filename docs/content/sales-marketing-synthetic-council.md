# The Starting Monday Synthetic Council

A panel of six advisors whose frameworks govern every piece of content we publish. Before anything goes live - blog post, LinkedIn post, landing page copy, email - it must survive their scrutiny.

---

## The Council Members

### Dave Gerhardt
**Area of expertise:** Marketing narrative and audience trust. B2B SaaS marketing, founder-led content, audience building, narrative-led demand creation.

**First principles:** Stories beat feature lists. Trust precedes conversion. Founder voice can become a distribution asset.

**Key questions he asks:**
- Is there a real story here, or a feature list dressed up as one?
- Does this sound like a founder who lived this problem, or a marketer who read about it?
- Is there a clear point of view the reader can agree or disagree with?
- Would someone share this because it made them think, or skip it because it sounds like every other B2B post?

---

### April Dunford
**Area of expertise:** Positioning and category clarity. B2B tech positioning, category framing, segmentation, sales story construction.

**First principles:** Customers need context to understand value. Positioning defines who should care and why. Differentiation requires a best-fit customer and a competitive alternative.

**Key questions she asks:**
- Does the reader immediately understand who this is for and who it is not for?
- What is the competitive alternative we are positioning against — and have we named it clearly?
- What unique attribute does Starting Monday have that no competitor can honestly claim?
- Would a CIO who is not actively searching understand why they should care?

---

### John McMahon
**Area of expertise:** Enterprise sales rigor. Enterprise SaaS sales leadership, qualification, pipeline inspection, revenue team coaching.

**First principles:** Complex sales are won by understanding pain, power, urgency, and the economic buyer. Qualification protects time and margin.

**Key questions he asks:**
- Does this content speak to a pain that is real, felt, and costs the executive something?
- Does it create urgency — a reason to act now, not eventually?
- Does it speak to the economic buyer (the executive in search) or to a proxy?
- Would this content make someone pick up the phone, or just nod and move on?

---

### Katelyn Bourgoin
**Area of expertise:** Pricing psychology and buyer motivation. Buyer psychology, customer triggers, jobs-to-be-done, perceived value.

**First principles:** People buy progress, relief, confidence, and status. Willingness to pay depends on perceived stakes and emotional value.

**Key questions she asks:**
- What is the emotional state of the reader at the moment they encounter this content?
- Does this speak to the anxiety of the search — or does it just describe the solution?
- Are we selling relief and progress, or features and functions?
- What switching moment does this content target — and does it meet the reader exactly there?

---

### Patrick Campbell
**Area of expertise:** SaaS monetization and packaging. Pricing strategy, subscription monetization, retention economics, value metrics.

**First principles:** Pricing is a high-leverage growth lever. Packaging should match segments and expansion paths. ARPU and retention are designed.

**Key questions he asks:**
- Does this content reinforce the value of the tier the reader is on or should be on?
- Does it create expansion intent — a reason to want more than the base plan?
- Are we communicating value in terms of outcomes the customer cares about, not features we built?
- Does this content support retention — does it remind users why they stay?

---

### Seth Godin
**Area of expertise:** Permission marketing, category storytelling, and audience trust. Tribe formation, smallest viable audience strategy, and remarkable positioning.

**First principles:** Permission beats interruption. People like us do things like this. Remarkable products and sharp audience focus create compounding word-of-mouth.

**Key questions he asks:**
- Are we earning permission and trust, or just trying to capture attention?
- Is this written for a smallest viable audience, or for everyone?
- What in this message is truly remarkable enough to be shared?
- Does this content strengthen category identity for executive opportunity intelligence?

## How to Use the Council

### Full Review (required for blog posts and major copy)

Run this prompt in Claude before publishing:

```
You are the Starting Monday Synthetic Council. Review the following content through the lens of each council member below.

For each member, answer:
1. What works — specific lines or decisions that align with their principles
2. What fails — specific lines or decisions that would draw their criticism
3. One concrete change they would demand before approving publication

Council members:
- Dave Gerhardt: narrative, founder voice, story over features
- April Dunford: positioning clarity, who it is for, competitive alternative
- John McMahon: pain, urgency, economic buyer, reason to act now
- Katelyn Bourgoin: emotional trigger, anxiety, progress and relief framing
- Patrick Campbell: value reinforcement, tier alignment, retention and expansion
- Seth Godin: permission marketing, smallest viable audience, category resonance

Starting Monday context: AI-powered job search platform for senior executives. Hero message: "The role was never posted. You found it anyway." Three tiers: Intelligence ($49/mo), Search ($199/mo), Executive ($499/mo). The platform handles research, tracking, and daily intelligence so the executive can focus on relationships and positioning.

Content to review:

[PASTE CONTENT HERE]
```

### Quick Review (for LinkedIn posts and emails)

Pick the two most relevant council members for the specific content type and run only their lenses. For LinkedIn posts: always run Gerhardt (is this worth sharing?) and Bourgoin (does this land on the right emotional trigger?).

## The Standard

Content that survives the council will be:
- Anchored in a specific, felt pain — not a generic problem
- Written from a founder who lived the search, not a vendor who sells to it
- Clear on who it is for before the second paragraph
- Compelling enough to share — not just accurate enough to publish
