# Starting Monday Content Standards

The definitive guide for everyone who creates or publishes content for Starting Monday. This governs blog posts, LinkedIn posts, landing page copy, emails, and any other public-facing writing.

---

## What We Are Building

Starting Monday is the operating system for the senior executive job search. It gives executives the intelligence infrastructure that senior searches require: company monitoring before roles post, AI-generated interview prep, pipeline tracking, and a daily briefing that keeps the search moving.

The platform handles research and accountability. The executive handles relationships and positioning. That is the division of labor — and it is the core of every message we send.

---

## Who We Are Writing For

**Primary:** Senior executives in active search or approaching one. CIOs, CTOs, CISOs, CDOs, COOs, VPs of Technology. Ten or more years of leadership experience. Their searches happen through networks, not job boards. They are managing 40-60 target companies, multiple search firm relationships, and a LinkedIn presence that must not telegraph desperation.

**Secondary:** Executive coaches whose clients are in the search. They are advisors looking for infrastructure that makes their sessions more strategic.

**Not for:** Job seekers at the manager or director level. Anyone who expects a job board. Anyone who is not already operating at or near the senior executive level.

Write to the primary audience. They are intelligent, time-constrained, and highly skeptical of generic advice. They have seen every job search tip that has ever been written. If the content does not tell them something they could not have assembled themselves, they will not read the second paragraph.

---

## The Standard: NYT Quality

Every piece of content — blog post, LinkedIn post, email — must be worthy of placement in the New York Times.

That means:
- **Specificity over generality.** "Most CIO searches take six to eighteen months" is a fact. "Most CIO searches take time" is not a sentence.
- **Evidence over assertion.** Show the logic. Do not just state the conclusion.
- **No hedging.** "It can sometimes be helpful to consider..." is not a sentence we publish.
- **No filler.** Every sentence must earn its place. If removing it does not change the meaning, remove it.
- **No AI tells.** No "delve into," "leverage," "in today's landscape," "it is worth noting," "navigate," "crucial," or any phrase that sounds like a language model asked to sound professional.
- **One strong idea per piece.** Not five mediocre ones.

The test: would a senior editor at the Times cut this paragraph? If yes, we cut it first.

## Voice and Style

See the full style guide in `docs/content/writing-style.md`. Core rules:

- Short sentences. Sequence them. Let each one land before the next one arrives.
- No em dashes. Use a period or restructure the sentence.
- Parallelism in lists. If the first item starts with a verb, all items start with a verb.
- Plain verbs. "Use" not "leverage." "Show" not "illustrate." "Find" not "discover."
- If-then framing where useful. "If you are watching forty companies manually, you are already behind."
- Write in the second person for instructional content. "You" not "executives should."
- The founder voice is an asset. Rich has lived this search. Content that draws on that experience is more credible than content that summarizes research.

## Content Types and Cadence

### Blog Posts
- **Frequency:** 2 per week
- **Length:** 800-1,200 words
- **Standard:** Full council review before publishing (see `docs/content/synthetic-council.md`)
- **Purpose:** SEO, authority building, inbound from Google. Each post targets a specific keyword cluster and answers a question a senior executive in search would actually type.
- **Tone:** Practitioner to practitioner. Not vendor to buyer.

### LinkedIn Posts (Liz publishes)
- **Frequency:** 1 per day, 5 days per week
- **Length:** 150-300 words for standard posts; longer for high-engagement narrative posts
- **Standard:** Quick council review — always Gerhardt + Bourgoin at minimum
- **Mix:** 3 original insights per week, 1 blog repurpose, 1 engagement post (question or observation)
- **Do not:** Announce features. Promote the product directly. Sound like an ad.
- **Do:** Share observations from the search. Tell stories from the executive transition experience. Point to blog posts as "here is the full version."

### Email (lifecycle)
- **Standard:** Full council review for new sequence templates; quick review for one-off sends
- **Tone:** Direct, personal, short. No newsletter formatting. No headers. Reads like an email from a person.

### Landing Pages
- **Standard:** Full council review. No exceptions.
- **Update frequency:** When user research, conversion data, or positioning shifts.

## The Council Review

Every blog post and major copy change must pass the Synthetic Council before publishing. The council consists of five advisors whose frameworks govern content quality, positioning clarity, buyer psychology, sales effectiveness, and pricing reinforcement.

Full process in `docs/content/synthetic-council.md`.

Quick reference — the five questions every piece of content must answer before publishing:

1. **Gerhardt:** Is there a real story here, or a feature list in disguise?
2. **Dunford:** Does the reader know in the first two paragraphs who this is for and who it is not for?
3. **McMahon:** Does this speak to a pain that costs the reader something — and create urgency to act?
4. **Bourgoin:** Does this land on the emotional state of an executive in transition — or does it float above it?
5. **Campbell:** Does this reinforce the value of the platform in terms of outcomes, not features?

## Liz's Publishing Workflow

### When a new blog post is ready:
1. Rich or Chris pushes the post to production (Railway auto-deploys)
2. Rich clicks "Notify Liz" in the admin panel (sends email with post details + draft LinkedIn copy)
3. Liz runs a quick council check on the LinkedIn copy using the prompt in `synthetic-council.md`
4. Liz posts on LinkedIn with any refinements — does not copy/paste verbatim
5. Liz monitors comments and responses for the first 48 hours

### LinkedIn posting cadence (weekly template):
- **Monday:** Insight from the week's blog post — pull the sharpest line and build a short post around it
- **Tuesday:** Original observation — something from the executive search world that starting Monday users would recognize
- **Wednesday:** Warm connection outreach batch — not a post, but a direct message sequence to 10-15 relevant connections
- **Thursday:** Question or poll — something that invites the target audience to engage
- **Friday:** Story — a brief narrative from the executive transition experience (anonymized if from a user)

### Sales Navigator outreach:
- Target: VP and above, currently employed but with tenure signals that suggest they may be approaching a transition (3-5 years in current role, company in flux)
- Message tone: Peer to peer. Not sales. Share a specific insight from the blog. Do not pitch the product in the first message.
- Sequence: Introduction + insight → follow-up with one more resource if no response → no third touch

## What We Do Not Publish

- Generic career advice that does not require executive-level experience to write
- Content that could appear on any job search platform
- Lists of tips without a central argument
- Anything that sounds like it was generated by AI and not edited by a person
- Feature announcements dressed up as thought leadership
- Content that promotes the platform before it has earned the reader's trust
