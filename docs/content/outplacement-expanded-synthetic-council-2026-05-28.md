## Consolidated pain point ranking from the expanded council

1. Cohort consistency breaks as programs scale across regions, counselors, and participant types.
2. Counselors lose high-value time rebuilding status instead of coaching strategy and decision quality.
3. Weekly execution decays after the early workshop phase, especially when follow-up ownership is unclear.
4. Program operations teams absorb avoidable cleanup work when there is no shared readiness standard.
5. Buyers struggle to see cohort-level momentum and intervention risk without manual reporting.
6. Procurement stalls when KPI claims, confidentiality boundaries, and decision rights are not board-safe.
7. Pilot approval weakens when rollout appears custom, founder-dependent, or operationally vague.
8. Counselors resist tools that sound like replacement instead of support.
9. Large firms need language tied to placement speed, service differentiation, and operating leverage, not generic productivity copy.
10. Standard outplacement still leaves a daily search-infrastructure gap between sessions.

## Rerun of revised outplacement sequence

Sequence evaluated:
- Initial: `A shared readiness standard for [Company]`
- Follow-up 1: `A shared readiness standard for [Company] cohorts`
- Follow-up 2: `A no-custom pilot for [Company] cohorts`
- Follow-up 3: `Close the loop for [Company] readiness?`

### Council verdict

- Enterprise operators favored the new sequence because it moved from generic "checklist" language to operational-standard language.
- Legal and procurement lenses approved the added proof caveat because it converts a risky claim into directional evidence.
- Counseling and coaching lenses preferred the new copy because it frames the product as protecting strategy time rather than automating away human judgment.
- Operations leaders rated follow-up 2 as the strongest second-touch because it neutralizes rollout-friction objections directly.
- The founder/category lens would still reject any version that gets too software-y or loses the dignity-of-transition posture.

### Brutally honest council grading

- Initial email: strongest message in the sequence. It balances pain, mechanism, and proof without sounding inflated.
- Follow-up 1: good, but only because it stays concrete. If list quality is weak, this will feel familiar and lose lift.
- Follow-up 2: best message for real buyers who own rollout or budget. It is less flashy, but more commercially serious.
- Follow-up 3: strong close-loop mail. High open probability, but reply depends heavily on whether the first two messages hit a real operating owner.

### Projected outcomes from expanded-council rerun

- Follow-up 1: projected open rate 50-58%; projected reply rate 8-12%.

## Recommendation

Use the revised four-email sequence in the canonical template engine.

Do not lead with generic checklist language alone. Lead with:
- shared readiness standard
- counselor yield
- coordinator cleanup reduction
- no-custom pilot path
- directional proof with explicit caveat

That is the set of messages the expanded council judged most buyable.
