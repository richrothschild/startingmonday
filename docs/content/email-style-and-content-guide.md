# Email Style and Content Guide

Purpose:

This guide is the default standard for all outbound emails.

Goal:

Every email should feel human, be easy to read in one pass, and drive a clear next step.

## Job of Every Email

1. Get opened.
2. Be understood quickly.
3. Get a reply.
4. Increase the chance the reader uses the product.

If one of these fails, the email failed.

## Voice Standard

1. Write like one person to one person.
2. Sound calm, direct, and useful.
3. Avoid framework labels inside body copy.
4. Prefer plain words over abstract terms.

Do not use labels such as:

- Trigger this week
- Choice
- State
- Signal
- Decision
- Action
- Proof

Keep the logic, not the labels.

## Structure Standard (Default)

Use four short paragraphs, then sign-off.

1. Context sentence: one natural sentence tied to recipient reality.
2. Decision sentence: one plain either or decision.
3. Proof sentence: one number plus one caveat.
4. CTA sentence: one yes or pass ask with one asset.

Rules:

1. One idea per sentence.
2. Keep most sentences between 8 and 16 words.
3. Keep each paragraph to one or two lines.
4. No more than one ask per email.

## Subject Line Standard

1. Be specific, not clever.
2. Include one context token.
3. Include one outcome token.
4. Target concise length where possible.

Examples:

- CIO first-call plan for Northstar Health
- Partner search first-touch plan for Pinnacle Search Partners
- A simple way to reduce prep drag between sessions

## Trigger Personalization Standard

Use dynamic inputs when available:

1. newsTrigger
2. postTrigger
3. profileTrigger

Priority order in rendering:

1. newsTrigger
2. postTrigger
3. profileTrigger
4. channel fallback sentence

Trigger content must be written as a natural sentence, not a label.

## Proof Standard

1. Keep one proof sentence.
2. Include denominator when available.
3. Include one caveat.
4. Avoid stacked claims.

Preferred pattern:

In our Jan-May 2026 pilot group (n=27), active users reached first qualified outreach in a median of 9 days; results vary by market and execution.

## CTA Standard

1. Use one binary CTA: yes or pass.
2. Ask for one asset only.
3. Make close-loop behavior explicit.

Preferred pattern:

If useful, reply yes and I will send [asset]. If not useful right now, reply pass and I will close the loop.

## Humanity Guardrails

Before send, check each draft:

1. Could a real person say this out loud?
2. Can the reader restate the ask in five seconds?
3. Does any sentence sound like a template instruction?
4. Is the tone respectful and low-pressure?

If any answer is no, rewrite.

## Channel Notes

Executives:

- Emphasize first-call quality and momentum risk.

Coaches:

- Emphasize recovered session time and lower prep drag.

Search firms:

- Emphasize first-touch quality and reduced partner review loops.

Outplacement:

- Emphasize consistent first-call quality across cohorts.

## Implementation Rule

This guide is the default for all future email templates, rewrites, and follow-ups unless a channel-specific policy overrides it.
