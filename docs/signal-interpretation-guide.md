# Signal Interpretation Guide

startingmonday.app | May 2026

---

A signal is a company event that changes the probability of an opportunity. Not a confirmation — a change in probability. The executive who reads signals well moves early, reaches out at the right moment, and arrives in conversation before a search is authorized. The one who waits for the job posting arrives with everyone else.

This guide explains what each signal type means in practice and how to use it.

---

## How to Read a Signal

Not every signal is relevant. Relevance depends on two things: whether the signal changes the likelihood of a need that fits your background, and whether you have or can develop a credible connection to that company.

When a signal fires, the first question is: does this change anything? A funding round at a company you have been watching for six months may change a lot — it often precedes infrastructure investment, leadership expansion, or a mandate that requires exactly what you bring. A funding round at a company where you have no fit and no connection changes nothing for your search.

The second question is: what is the right action, and how quickly? Some signals have a short window. A departing CIO is being replaced. The search has started or will start within 30 days. A candidate who reaches out to their contact at that company in week one is having a fundamentally different conversation than one who reaches out in week six. Other signals — a new board member, a strategic announcement — have a longer fuse. They signal that a change is coming without telling you exactly when.

## Signal Types and What They Mean

Funding (Series B, C, growth equity, PE investment): The company is scaling or transforming. New capital typically precedes organizational investment — leadership hiring, technology build-out, operational change. The window between funding announcement and leadership search authorization is 30 to 90 days for companies that need to move quickly. For PE investments specifically, the new ownership almost always brings operational changes that affect technology leadership.

Executive departure (CIO, CTO, CFO, COO): A search is underway or imminent. The company has a gap at a leadership level and needs to fill it. The question is whether the role is being backfilled, restructured, or elevated. Your contact at the company knows — or knows who knows — and the right outreach in the first two weeks of a departure announcement produces a conversation no recruiter introduction can replicate. Move within ten business days.

Executive hire (new CEO, new CFO, new PE operating partner): The new leader is assessing their team. New CEOs in particular are evaluating technology leadership within the first 90 to 120 days. A new CFO often brings scrutiny of technology investment and cost structure. A new PE operating partner is building a thesis for the portfolio company. These are not departure signals — they are inflection signals. The right contact is someone who knows the incoming leader or has worked with them before.

Acquisition: The integration need is immediate. Companies that acquire need someone who can manage integration complexity. If your record includes M&A technology integration, an acquisition announcement at a target company is a high-priority signal. The window is short — integration leadership is identified early in the process, often before the deal closes publicly.

Expansion (new market, new geography, new product line): Growth creates organizational stress and often precedes leadership change. An expansion announcement signals that the company's current structure is being tested. The relevant question is whether the expansion requires technology infrastructure that a new leader would build or lead.

Layoffs: The situation is more nuanced than the headline. A company reducing headcount in one division while investing in another is a restructuring story, not a decline story. Layoffs in a PE-backed company often precede a technology modernization mandate — the reduction in operational cost is frequently followed by investment in the technology that makes the new structure work. Read the accompanying announcement carefully.

IPO: The pre-IPO technology build-out and the post-IPO governance requirements both create leadership needs. The pre-IPO window is 12 to 18 months before the event; the post-IPO window is the 6 to 12 months after, when public company reporting and compliance infrastructure needs to be in place. Know where the company is in its IPO timeline when the signal fires.

New product or award: Weaker signal, but still worth noting. A major product launch signals organizational investment and attention. A significant award can indicate where leadership attention is focused. These are background signals that give you something specific to reference in outreach, not action signals on their own.

## The Outreach Angle

Every signal in Starting Monday includes an outreach angle — a suggested framing for why this signal is relevant to your search and how to use it in a conversation. Read it. Then decide whether it applies to your specific situation and adapt it.

The outreach angle is a starting point, not a script. The best outreach takes the angle and makes it specific to your relationship with the contact and your record. "I saw the acquisition announcement — I've run four integrations in exactly this stack and wanted to share a perspective" is better than any generic version of the same thought.

---

## When Not to Act on a Signal

Not every signal is actionable. If you have no connection to the company — no contact, no mutual connection, and no credible reason for cold outreach — a signal tells you to add the company to your list and start building toward a connection. It does not justify a cold message that says "I saw your company announced X, and I happen to be available." That message produces nothing.

Similarly, a signal at a company you have assessed as a poor fit is informational, not directional. The signal may change your fit assessment — an acquisition at a company that was previously too small, for example — but if the core fit is not there, the signal is still background noise.

Use signals to time and sharpen outreach you were already planning. Do not use them to justify outreach you had no other reason to send.
