# Interview Day Cheat Sheet

startingmonday.app | May 2026

---

This is for the hour before the call. Not a guide to interview strategy — the prep brief handles that. This is the checklist that prevents the avoidable mistakes.

---

## The Hour Before

Read the prep brief from top to bottom. Not the version from last week — the current version, which reflects the most recent signals and any documents you have added. The brief is most useful the day of the call, not the day before.

Identify the two or three things you want the interviewer to know about you by the end of the call. Not everything you could say — two or three things. The executive who walks in knowing exactly what they want to establish walks out having established it. The executive who walks in hoping the conversation surfaces the right topics usually leaves having talked about the wrong ones.

Write down the one question you most want answered today. Every first call has something the other side wants to assess and something you need to find out. Know what yours is before you dial.

---

## Technical and Environment

Test your connection ten minutes before the call, not two. If something is wrong with your audio, your login, or the link they sent, you have time to fix it or alert the other party. Discovering this at the start of the call tells the interviewer something about how you manage high-stakes situations.

Use a room with a closed door. Background noise is a distraction. A dog barking or a child entering during a CEO-level call is recoverable once, but it costs you something. Do not start from behind.

Have a pen and paper. Not because you will take extensive notes, but because writing something down signals engagement and gives you somewhere to put a thought you want to come back to rather than interrupting the flow to say it.

---

## The Opening Two Minutes

Most calls begin with the same three minutes: pleasantries, a brief framing of why you are here, and then a question about your background or your interest. Know how you are going to handle these two minutes before they happen.

Your opening summary — the answer to "Tell me about yourself" or "Walk me through your background" — should take ninety seconds and land on the thing most relevant to this company's situation. It is not a chronological walkthrough. It is a curated argument for why you are the right conversation to be having.

Practice it aloud once before the call. Not because it should sound rehearsed — it should not — but because saying it aloud is different from thinking it, and you want to hear how it lands before the call, not during it.

---

## During the Call

Ask the question you prepared. Not as a generic placeholder — as a real question that shows you have been paying attention. "Given the CFO transition that was announced last month, how is the board thinking about technology's role in supporting the integration?" is a real question. "What does success look like in this role?" is not.

Listen for the pause after you answer a question. When an interviewer pauses after your answer, they are often deciding whether to probe deeper or move on. Let them decide. Do not fill the pause with more. If they want more, they will ask for it.

When you do not know something, say so directly. "I don't have direct experience with that specific scenario, but here is how I would approach it given what I have done in adjacent situations." That is a stronger answer than a rambling attempt to cover a gap by talking around it.

---

## The Last Five Minutes

Every call ends with the same question or a version of it: do you have any questions for us? Have at least two real ones. Not research questions — you should have done that research already. Questions about what the company is trying to solve that only someone inside the room can answer.

The last thing you say should not trail off. Close with something that signals you are genuinely interested and gives them a natural next step. "I'd like to continue this conversation — what would be helpful to you as a next step?" is direct. It moves the process forward without overplaying your hand.

---

## After the Call

Log the conversation in your system within two hours. Write down who was on the call, the key things you learned, what they seemed most interested in from your background, and any follow-up you committed to. Memory decays fast. The note you write today is the context for every call that follows.

Send a brief follow-up note within 24 hours. Not an essay — three sentences. What you appreciated about the conversation, the thing you found most interesting from their side, and your continued interest. Do not use "It was a pleasure meeting you" as the opening line. Everyone does that. Start with something specific.

Set the follow-up reminder before you close the tab.
