# Market Reassessment: Executives, Coaches, and Outplacement

Date: 2026-05-28
Status: Reconstructed from available planning references and linked strategy artifacts

## Executive Summary

Starting Monday is not being reassessed as a single broad job-search product. It is being reassessed as a multi-motion system with three distinct go-to-market paths:

1. Direct executive search support for VP, C-suite, and board-track candidates.
2. Coach-led distribution, where executive coaches use the product as the operating layer behind their guidance.
3. Outplacement and transition-firm distribution, where firms package the product into client programs.

The reassessment conclusion is that the product has real pull in all three motions, but not equal maturity. The direct executive motion has the clearest user pain and the highest product credibility, but it is also the hardest to scale without disciplined onboarding and trust. The coach motion appears to be the best wedge for distribution. The outplacement motion is the best structured buyer path for repeatable pilots.

The core risk is not demand in the abstract. The core risk is execution: if the operating system is weak, none of the motions will convert reliably.

## Market Framing

This reassessment assumes the market should be treated as a layered set of buyer problems rather than one uniform persona.

### Direct Executive Motion

This motion serves senior candidates who are running an active search themselves. They want monitoring, preparation, follow-up discipline, and an organized pipeline. They are already motivated, but they need structure.

Primary characteristics:

1. High willingness to pay when the product saves time or improves outcome quality.
2. Strong sensitivity to trust, confidentiality, and signal quality.
3. High expectation that the product understands executive-level search behavior rather than generic job hunting.

Assessment:

1. PMF is emerging.
2. The pain is real and immediate.
3. Conversion is fragile if onboarding is shallow or the product feels noisy.

### Coach Partner Motion

This motion serves executive coaches, search advisors, and adjacent consultants who already guide candidates through a search but need an operational layer.

Primary characteristics:

1. The coach retains the relationship and judgment role.
2. Starting Monday handles monitoring, prep briefs, tracking, and routine follow-through.
3. The coach becomes more effective without being replaced.

Assessment:

1. This is the strongest wedge.
2. The value proposition is easiest to explain.
3. Distribution is more scalable because coaches can introduce the product into multiple client relationships.

### Outplacement Motion

This motion serves transition firms, employer-sponsored outplacement programs, and related partner channels.

Primary characteristics:

1. Buyers care about measurable client progress and safe program governance.
2. Procurement, legal, and trust artifacts matter more than in the direct motion.
3. Pilot outcomes have to be visible fast enough to justify continued use.

Assessment:

1. Early pilot fit is real.
2. The motion is operationally attractive because it creates repeatable cohort delivery.
3. Conversion depends on clear claims, strong onboarding, and decision-grade reporting.

## Segment PMF Snapshot

The reassessment landed on three different PMF states.

1. Executive direct motion: emerging PMF, but fragile.
2. Coach partner motion: strongest wedge potential.
3. Outplacement motion: early pilot fit.

The important distinction is that these are not three separate products. They are three distribution and packaging paths for the same core engine. If the engine is weak, the channels will all fail differently.

## Revenue Direction

The market reassessment was used to frame revenue direction at a planning level rather than as a fixed forecast.

The working assumptions were:

1. Direct subscriptions can create early product learning and proof of demand.
2. Coach-led access can lower acquisition friction and improve retention through repeated use.
3. Outplacement and partner deals can drive larger, more predictable contract value once the product is operationally credible.

The practical conclusion is that partner-led mix should grow over time, but only after direct usage has sharpened the product and the operating model is dependable.

Revenue sensitivity is driven more by churn and activation quality than by raw TAM. A large addressable market does not matter if users do not reach value quickly or if partners cannot run the workflow confidently.

## Trust and Operating Reality

The largest bottleneck is not market size. It is operating readiness.

The product sits in a trust-heavy category:

1. Candidates are sharing career history, search intent, and confidential transition context.
2. Coaches and firms need assurance that the system will not create noise or expose sensitive information.
3. Buyers need confidence that the product supports better decisions, not just more activity.

That means product-market fit has to be enforced through operating discipline, not just feature development.

## 7-Layer Operating Enforcement

The reassessment introduced a hard operating model that treats the product and the organization as a governed system.

The enforcement model has four main elements:

1. Mandatory gate logic from intake through outcome review.
2. 7-layer scorecards attached to changes and reviews.
3. Governance cadence with named owners and remediation dates.
4. Decision logs that make pass/fail/hold explicit rather than informal.

The reason for this layer is simple: executive and partner buyers do not forgive weak trust posture. If the product feels casual, the market will read that as risk.

## Execution Implications

The reassessment implies three near-term implementation priorities.

### 1. Strengthen Executive Direct UX

The direct motion needs an onboarding flow that quickly captures search persona, target companies, positioning, and transition context.

Key requirement:

1. The product must produce immediate value after onboarding.
2. The user should see that the system understands their search shape.
3. The interface should reduce uncertainty, not add it.

### 2. Package a Coach-Friendly Operating Layer

The coach motion needs a way to add clients, review prep, monitor progress, and create repeatable habits without replacing the coach's judgment.

Key requirement:

1. Coaches need visible client progress.
2. They need prep briefs and tracking support.
3. They need a low-friction way to introduce the product into sessions.

### 3. Make Outplacement Safe to Pilot

The outplacement motion needs buyer-safe claims, governance artifacts, and a clear pilot-to-scale path.

Key requirement:

1. Buyers need to see how the pilot works.
2. Legal and procurement need confidence in the claims and data boundaries.
3. The pilot must produce measurable behavior changes early.

## Risks

The main risks in this reassessment are:

1. Overestimating the readiness of direct executive users to self-serve without a strong guided onboarding model.
2. Underestimating the trust and procurement burden in partner channels.
3. Treating channel expansion as a substitute for product quality and operating discipline.
4. Building too far ahead of validated workflow behavior.

## Bottom Line

The market is viable across executive, coach, and outplacement motions, but the winning path is not to treat all three as equal at once. The coach motion is the best wedge, the outplacement motion is the best structured scaling channel, and the direct executive motion is the best product proof.

If the operating model holds, the product can move from useful tool to trusted system. If the operating model slips, the market will read that as a lack of seriousness regardless of feature depth.
