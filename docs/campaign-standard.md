# The Campaign Standard
## A Writing Framework for Starting Monday

Every document produced for Starting Monday is scored against this framework before delivery. Nothing leaves without passing. The standard is simple: would the editor of the New York Times let this through? Would Jamie Dimon, reading it cold, find anything to dismiss?

---

## Part 1: The Kill List

These are automatic failures. When found, delete or rewrite. There is no editing around them.

### AI Writing Tells

These patterns mark a document as machine-generated. A sophisticated reader — an investor, a CIO, a hiring partner — will notice before they notice anything else. The document loses credibility before the argument begins.

Em dashes used as dramatic pause or aside. Arrows (→) used to show sequence. Asterisks for emphasis. Bold text within prose paragraphs (not headers). Gratuitous parallel construction that becomes mechanical. Three-part lists used as a stylistic tic. Every paragraph beginning with "The." Colons used to introduce lists that could be a single sentence. Transitional summaries ("In summary," "To recap," "In conclusion"). Sentences opening with "Additionally," "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Importantly." Rhetorical questions as section openers. Numbered lists for items that are not actually sequential. Bullet points substituted for prose in any narrative document.

The test: ask "Would the New York Times allow this?" The Times does not use arrows. It does not bold phrases mid-paragraph for emphasis. It does not open sections with rhetorical questions. It uses bullets and numbered lists sparingly, only when the content is genuinely list-like, and never as a substitute for prose.

### Corporate Buzzwords

Each of the following is an automatic delete. Replace with a specific claim or cut entirely.

leverage (as a verb), synergies, going forward, at the end of the day, best-in-class, world-class, innovative, cutting-edge, robust, seamless, holistic, ecosystem, stakeholders, deliverables, actionable, impactful, learnings, circle back, touch base, move the needle, low-hanging fruit, bandwidth (for human capacity), pivot, thought leader, disruptive, game-changer, deep dive, pain points, journey (metaphorical), space (for market or industry), landscape (for environment or market), whitespace (business use), unlock (for features or benefits), empower, democratize, utilize (use "use"), ensure (use "make sure"), prior to (use "before"), in order to (use "to"), due to the fact that (use "because"), exciting (used to describe business outcomes), amazing, incredible, unprecedented (without evidence), unparalleled (almost never true), transformative (without evidence), unique (almost never true)

### Weasel Words and Hedges

These signal a writer who is not confident in the claim. Either make the claim or do not.

arguably, somewhat, generally, often, typically, usually (when used to hedge rather than to state fact), it could be argued, one might say, in some ways, in a sense, to some extent, perhaps (when not genuinely uncertain), this suggests, this indicates (when the evidence says something directly)

## Part 2: Universal Principles

These apply to every document regardless of type or audience. They are not guidelines. They are requirements.

### Principle 1: The opening earns the read.

The first sentence is a promise. If it does not tell the reader why this document matters to them, they will not read the second. The lede is not a warm-up. It is the argument. Start with the most important thing, not the context for the most important thing.

Bad: "Executive job search is a complex process that requires careful attention to many factors."

Good: "The role you want is already being filled. It was never posted."

The test: cover everything after the first sentence. Does the first sentence alone justify the reader's continued attention? If not, rewrite it until it does.

### Principle 2: Every sentence earns its place or is cut.

Read every sentence and ask: if this sentence disappears, does anything important disappear with it? If the answer is no, cut it. A document is not improved by length. It is improved by density.

The test: identify the three sentences in the document that contribute the least. Cut them. If the document is better without them, cut five more.

### Principle 3: Specificity is credibility.

Abstractions signal uncertainty. Concrete details signal mastery. Never write "executives struggle with this process" when you can write "most executive candidates abandon their tracking spreadsheet by week six." Numbers, names, timelines, and outcomes are the currency of credibility. When you cannot be specific, ask why. Usually the reason is that the claim is not actually defensible.

Bad: "The platform generates significant time savings for users preparing for interviews."

Good: "The brief takes 60 seconds to generate. The manual research equivalent takes four hours and rarely goes as deep."

### Principle 4: Active voice, specific subject.

The subject of the sentence performs the action. If you cannot name who does what, rewrite until you can. Passive voice is not wrong. It is a choice — and it is almost always the wrong choice.

Bad: "A prep brief is generated by the platform using company data and the candidate's profile."

Good: "Starting Monday builds the brief from the company's scan results, recent signals, attached documents, and your resume."

### Principle 5: One idea per paragraph.

When a paragraph contains two ideas, it contains zero ideas clearly. Split it. A paragraph is a unit of argument, not a unit of length. When the idea is done, the paragraph is done, regardless of how many sentences it contains.

### Principle 6: Vary sentence length deliberately.

Short sentences create emphasis. They stop the reader. They make the next sentence land harder. Longer sentences, when the subject requires the reader to hold multiple pieces simultaneously, demonstrate that the writer understands complexity and has not flattened it. Alternate between them. Never write three long sentences in a row. Never write five short ones.

Bad: "The executive job search is complex. It requires tracking. It requires preparation. It requires follow-up. Most people fail at this."

Good: "The executive job search is not complicated in theory. You know who you want to work for, and you know what you bring. The collapse happens in execution — the tracking that falls behind, the follow-up that never goes out, the call you walk into underprepared because you had forty-five minutes instead of four hours."

(Note: the em dash in the exemplar above is a deliberate exception — used once, for a specific rhythmic effect, in a sentence where no other construction works as well. The Kill List rule is against habitual em dashes, not surgical ones. Use this exception fewer than once per document.)

### Principle 7: The ending matters as much as the opening.

Documents trail off when the writer runs out of things to say. The final sentence should be the hardest one to cut. It should crystallize the argument, close the loop on the opening, or issue the clearest possible call to act. A document that ends well is remembered. A document that trails off is not.

Bad: "Starting Monday is the platform that helps executives manage their search effectively. We hope you find it useful."

Good: "The candidate who walks in most prepared wins. That is what Starting Monday is for."

### Principle 8: Never write down to the reader.

Assume the reader is as intelligent as the most intelligent person likely to read this document. Do not explain what they already know. Do not use transitions that imply they cannot follow logic without a signpost. Treat them as a peer. An investor brief written for a smart 25-year-old analyst who knows nothing about executive search will lose the 55-year-old managing partner before the third paragraph.

### Principle 9: Facts are sacred.

Every factual claim is verifiable. If a claim cannot be verified, it is either labeled as the author's view or removed. "Most executive candidates" is acceptable when it reflects genuine observation. "Studies show" without a citation is not acceptable. "Up to" metrics are not acceptable when a specific number is available.

### Principle 10: Read it aloud before it is done.

If you stumble reading a sentence aloud, the sentence is wrong. If a paragraph makes you run out of breath, it is too long. If a word sounds wrong said plainly, find a simpler word. This is not optional.

## Part 3: The Reader Test

Before any document is delivered, answer these three questions. If any cannot be answered with a specific, concrete response, the document is not done.

**What does the reader know after reading this that they did not know before?**

Not "they understand our product better." Name the specific knowledge. "The reader now knows that career pages at their target companies are scanned three times a week and that matches are flagged before they appear on LinkedIn." That is a specific answer. "They understand our value proposition better" is not.

**What does the reader feel after reading this that they did not feel before?**

Name the emotion and identify what in the document produces it. "The reader feels the urgency of information asymmetry — that others are getting to the right opportunities earlier — and the relief that a specific system exists to close that gap." If the document produces no feeling, it will produce no action.

**What does the reader do differently because of this document?**

Name the action. "The reader signs up for the 30-day trial." "The reader forwards this to a peer in search." "The reader walks into a salary negotiation with different information than they would have had." If the document produces knowledge and feeling but no action, it is interesting. It is not effective.

## Part 4: Audience Calibration

The universal principles apply everywhere. The register shifts by audience and purpose.

**Investor brief**: Authoritative, data-anchored, without hype. The reader is skeptical by training. Every assertion that cannot survive scrutiny should be cut before they find it. The investor brief that sounds like a pitch deck has already failed.

**Executive narrative documents** (business plan, persona documents, marketing plan): Confident and direct. Write as a peer. Do not soften claims. Do not hedge. Say the hard thing plainly. Jack Welch called this candor. He was right.

**User-facing guides** (onboarding, playbooks, cheat sheets): Practical, direct, warm without being cheerful. The reader is under pressure. They do not want to be encouraged. They want to know what to do. Give them exactly that and nothing more.

**Marketing copy** (one-pager, outplacement pitch): Punchy, with earned confidence. The claim must arrive before the proof. Short sentences dominate. Product confidence comes from specificity, not from superlatives.

**Reference documents** (architecture, roadmap, template): Structured and precise. Tables are preferable to prose when presenting comparative or sequential information. Lists are appropriate when the content is genuinely list-like. Prose sections within these documents still follow all universal principles.

**Format rules by document type:**

Narrative documents use prose paragraphs. Lists appear only when the content is genuinely list-like and prose would obscure the structure. A reference to three things in a sentence is a sentence, not a list.

Reference documents use structured format. Tables are preferred over bullets for comparative information. Headers organize sections. Prose sections obey the universal principles.

Marketing documents favor brevity above all else. Every word is tested against whether it earns its space in the document.

## Part 5: The Scoring Protocol

This is the process run on every document before it is delivered.

**Step 1: Kill List scan.**

Read top to bottom looking for every instance of a Kill List item. Flag each one with its exact location. Count the total. More than three instances in a document under 1,000 words indicates a structural problem, not a copy problem. The document requires rewriting, not editing.

**Step 2: Principle check, paragraph by paragraph.**

For each paragraph:
Does the first sentence carry the full weight of the paragraph?
Does the paragraph contain more than one idea?
Does every sentence earn its place?
Are all subjects and verbs active and specific?

Flag failures with exact location: "Paragraph 3, sentence 2: passive voice. 'Is generated by the platform' should be 'Starting Monday generates.'"

**Step 3: Reader test.**

Answer all three questions explicitly and specifically. When a question cannot be answered concretely, identify which part of the document creates the gap and revise.

**Step 4: Audience calibration check.**

Confirm the register matches the document type. Confirm format rules are respected: narrative documents are in prose; reference documents use appropriate structure; marketing documents are tight.

**Step 5: Read aloud.**

Read the complete document aloud. Mark every sentence that causes a stumble, a lost breath, or sounds mechanical. Revise before delivering.

**The delivery gate**: A document is ready when the Kill List scan finds zero instances, every flagged principle failure has been resolved, the Reader Test produces specific concrete answers to all three questions, and the read-aloud produces no stumbles.

## Part 6: Exemplars

One bad version. One revised version. The distance between them is the standard.

**The opening (Principle 1)**

Bad: "Starting Monday is an AI-powered career search platform designed to help senior executives navigate the complexities of the modern job market."

Good: "The role you want is already being filled. It was never posted."

**Specificity (Principle 3)**

Bad: "The platform saves users significant time on interview preparation."

Good: "The brief takes 60 seconds to generate. The manual research equivalent takes four hours and rarely goes as deep."

**Active voice (Principle 4)**

Bad: "A prep brief is generated by the platform using company data and the candidate's profile."

Good: "Starting Monday builds the brief from the company's scan results, recent signals, attached documents, and your resume."

**Sentence rhythm (Principle 6)**

Bad: "The executive job search is complex. It requires tracking. It requires preparation. It requires follow-up. Most people fail at this."

Good: "The executive job search is not complicated in theory. You know who you want to work for, and you know what you bring. The collapse happens in execution — the tracking that falls behind, the follow-up that never goes out, the call you walk into underprepared because you had forty-five minutes instead of four hours."

**Replacing bullets with prose**

Bad:
The platform offers:
- Career page scanning three times a week
- AI-generated prep briefs
- Company intelligence signals

Good: "The platform scans career pages at your target companies three times a week, generates prep briefs from the intersection of their data and your record, and surfaces company intelligence signals the moment they appear."

**Removing a weasel word**

Bad: "Starting Monday arguably gives candidates an edge in the early stages of a search."

Good: "Starting Monday surfaces roles before they are posted. The candidate who knows first has an edge that does not exist at all if they find out the same day as everyone else."

**Replacing corporate vocabulary**

Bad: "We leverage AI to empower executives to proactively engage with their target ecosystem."

Good: "Starting Monday uses AI to prepare executives for conversations before those conversations are possible anywhere else."

**The ending (Principle 7)**

Bad: "We believe Starting Monday can be a valuable tool for executives in search. Thank you for your consideration."

Good: "The candidate who walks in most prepared wins. That is what Starting Monday is for."

## Appendix: The Standard in One Sentence

Write what is true. Cut what is not needed. Say it plainly. Make the reader act.
