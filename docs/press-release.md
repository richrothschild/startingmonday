# Starting Monday — Working Backward Press Release

*Written in the Jeff Bezos style: start with the customer outcome, written as if it already happened. If you can't write this paragraph clearly, the product definition isn't done. Revise the product until this paragraph is true and proud.*

---

## The Placed Executive — What Starting Monday Did That Nothing Else Could Have Done

David Chen had been a CIO at a Fortune 500 retailer for four years when his company was acquired. He knew the search was coming six months before anyone said a word. Using Starting Monday, he had already built a target list of 23 companies, tracked leadership departures and funding announcements at each one, and identified a pattern at a mid-market healthcare technology company — a new PE sponsor, a new CFO, and a CIO role that had not been posted but was inevitable. He had two contacts at the company from a prior engagement. He reached out to both, framed around the PE transition, and had an informal conversation before the search was ever authorized. When the retained firm called him four weeks later, he had already been in the room. He received an offer that was 40 percent above his prior compensation and turned down two others. The search took 11 weeks from the moment he learned of his acquisition to the moment he signed. The national average for a CIO search is 8 to 12 months. What Starting Monday gave David was not a job board. It was the intelligence and infrastructure to run a campaign the way a search firm runs one — with signals, relationships, and positioning already in place before the search was ever formalized.

---

## What This Press Release Tells Us About the Product

Every feature decision should be measured against this story:

1. **Signal intelligence before posting** — David knew before the role existed. If our signals don't surface that pattern, we haven't done our job.
2. **Contact activation** — He had contacts at the company and knew to reach out. If our contact tracking doesn't surface "you have a connection here — now is the time," we're a passive tool.
3. **Positioning already assembled** — He walked in framed around the PE transition. The prep brief exists for this moment.
4. **Speed advantage** — 11 weeks vs. 8-12 months. The time compression is the metric. If users are not landing faster than the national average, something is wrong.
5. **Multiple offers** — He turned two down. The goal is not to land a job. The goal is to be in a position to choose.

---

## The Anti-Press-Release (What We Don't Want)

*This is the outcome we are building against:*

Mark was a VP of IT who had been ready for a CIO role for three years. When his company restructured, he spent four months applying to posted roles on LinkedIn, getting ghosted by ATS systems, and waiting for recruiters to call back. He interviewed twice, both for roles below his level, and accepted one of them eight months after his separation — at the same compensation he had left. He never knew that three companies in his target space had been quietly searching for a CIO during that period. He had contacts at two of them. He never reached out because he didn't know the searches existed.

If a Starting Monday user has that outcome, we failed. Not because the search was hard — it is always hard. Because the intelligence existed and we didn't surface it. Because the contacts existed and we didn't activate them. Because the timing was there and we didn't see it.

## The Placed Page Version (Arc 9 Moment)

*This is the paragraph we want every placed user to feel they could write:*

"I tracked 18 companies for three months before my search was ever announced. When I told people I was in search, I already had conversations in progress at four of them. I didn't apply to a single posted role. I was placed through a relationship I had been warming for six weeks. Starting Monday didn't find me a job. It made sure I was already in motion when the right conversation started."

---

## Product Definition Test

Before building any feature, ask: does this make the David Chen story more likely? If it doesn't, it belongs below the line.

Before shipping any copy, ask: does this speak to the person who will become David Chen, or to the person in the anti-press-release? If it speaks to the latter with urgency and fear, rewrite it.
