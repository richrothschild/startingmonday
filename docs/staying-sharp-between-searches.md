# Staying Sharp Between Searches

startingmonday.app | May 2026

---

Most executives treat the search as a sprint — they work it intensely while they need to, and the moment they accept an offer, everything stops. The target list goes cold, the contacts go unattended, and the market intelligence stops flowing. Three years later, they are starting from scratch.

This is the wrong model. The best time to build the foundation for your next search is immediately after your last one, when the relationships are warm, your positioning is fresh, and you know exactly what you are looking for.

---

## What Staying Sharp Actually Means

It does not mean running a search while employed. It means maintaining the infrastructure of a search without the urgency — watching the right companies, keeping relationships warm, and continuing to build the record that makes the next search faster and better positioned than the last one.

The executive who stays sharp between searches is never starting cold. When the moment comes — a restructuring, a better mandate, a company they have been watching for two years — they already have context on the target, contacts at the company, and a clear positioning narrative. The search that takes seven months when starting from nothing takes three months from this position.

---

## The Weekly Habit

Thirty minutes a week is enough to stay sharp during an active role. Review your signal digest. Note anything that moved at the companies you care about. Follow up with one contact from your professional network. That is the practice.

Not every week will produce something actionable. Most weeks nothing fires. The practice is not about finding something to act on — it is about maintaining the visibility into your market that makes action possible when the moment requires it.

---

## Keeping Relationships Warm

The contact who helped you in your last search will help you in your next one, but only if the relationship did not go cold in between. The most common failure in professional networking is letting relationships exist only when you need them. The people who maintain them continuously are the ones who get the calls when a mandate opens.

Staying in touch does not require elaborate effort. A brief note when you see something relevant to a contact's work, a brief catch-up call once or twice a year with your five closest professional advocates, a LinkedIn comment that adds something to a conversation they are having publicly. These are small investments. The return on them, when measured over a career, is not small.

---

## Building the Accomplishment Record

Every mandate you carry in your current role is material for the next search. The problem is that executives accumulate accomplishments in real time and then struggle to articulate them under pressure — either because the details are vague in memory or because they never made the specific outcome visible.

The discipline of staying sharp includes documenting accomplishments while they are fresh. When a project closes, write down the mandate, the approach, the team, the outcome, and the metric. A 200-word note written in the week after a major milestone is worth ten hours of effort trying to reconstruct it eighteen months later. These notes become the material for your resume, your strategy brief, and the specific answers you need in interviews.

---

## Watching the Right Companies

The companies on your watch list from the last search are still worth watching. Some of them will have moved on from the moment that made them interesting. Others will have evolved in ways that make the next mandate more relevant, not less.

Keep eight to ten companies under passive monitoring. Review the list once a quarter and update it — add the companies that have become more interesting, remove the ones that have drifted from your target. The career page scans run regardless. When something fires, you will see it.

The benefit of continuous monitoring over the two or three years between searches is not obvious until the moment it matters. The executive who has been watching a company for 26 months when the CIO departure is announced has context no newcomer can match. They know the company's priorities, the signals that fired over the past two years, and often have a contact or two who remembers their last conversation. That context is an advantage that cannot be manufactured in 48 hours.

---

## After the Offer

Start the post-search practice in the first two weeks after accepting an offer. Send thank-you notes to every person who helped you in the search — every introductory call, every recruiter conversation, every contact who connected you to someone. This is not optional. These people invested time and credibility in you. Acknowledge it specifically.

Update your accomplishment record with the key outcomes from the search itself: how long it took, what you learned about your market, what worked, what you would do differently. The search is a professional experience worth understanding, not just completing.

Set a calendar reminder six months out to review your company watch list and contact tracker. By that point you will be fully inside your new role and the search will feel distant. The reminder is the forcing function that keeps the infrastructure alive until the habit is established.

---

## The Long View

Most executives will run two or three significant searches over the course of their career. The candidates who navigate each one well are not always the most credentialed or the most connected in the traditional sense. They are the ones who maintained the infrastructure of a search continuously, so that when the moment arrived, they were not building from nothing.

The search that starts from scratch takes a year. The search that starts from a maintained foundation takes four months. That difference compounds over a career into a meaningful gap in outcomes, compensation, and the quality of the mandates available to you.

Stay sharp. It costs almost nothing in the years when you do not need it and pays back in the one when you do.
